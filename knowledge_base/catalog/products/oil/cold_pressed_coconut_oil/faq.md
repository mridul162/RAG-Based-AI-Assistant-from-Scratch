### 1. What exactly is "cold pressed" coconut oil — and how is it different from regular coconut oil?

Cold pressing extracts oil from fresh, mature coconut flesh using mechanical pressure only, without applying external heat or chemical solvents. The oil never exceeds approximately 40–49°C during extraction, which preserves the natural aroma, flavour, and bioactive compounds (including MCTs, polyphenols, and lauric acid) that are otherwise degraded or removed during conventional refining.

"Regular" coconut oil sold in most Bangladeshi grocery shops is typically Refined, Bleached, and Deodorised (RBD) oil. It is produced using high heat (150–230°C) and chemical solvents, which results in a neutral-smelling, flavourless product with significantly reduced nutrient content. RBD oil is cheaper and has a longer shelf life, but it is not equivalent to cold pressed oil in terms of nutritional profile or sensory quality.

### 2. How do I know the oil I receive is genuinely cold pressed and not refined oil in disguise?

There are several practical checks you can do at home:

- **Aroma:** Genuine cold pressed coconut oil has a natural, mild, fresh coconut scent when you open the bottle. Refined oil is essentially odourless. If it smells like nothing, it has been deodorised.
- **Solidification test:** Place a small amount in the refrigerator for 20–30 minutes. Genuine cold pressed oil solidifies into a pure, uniform white solid. Adulterated or blended oil may solidify unevenly, appear greyish, or leave a greasy residue after fully melting.
- **Taste:** Cold pressed oil has a clean, mild coconut flavour with no bitterness or chemical aftertaste. Refined oil is tasteless.
- **Appearance:** At temperatures above ~24°C, genuine cold pressed oil should be clear to water-white in liquid form — not yellow or murky.

For higher confidence, you can request a Certificate of Analysis (COA) from the supplier showing Free Fatty Acid (FFA) content (should be ≤0.5%) and lauric acid percentage (should be 45–55%).

### 3. The oil has solidified in the bottle. Is it spoiled?

No — this is completely normal and expected. Cold pressed coconut oil solidifies at approximately 24°C and below. During cooler months, or if stored in an air-conditioned room, the oil will turn into a white solid. This is a natural property of coconut oil's high saturated fat content and is actually a positive indicator of purity.

To melt it, simply place the bottle in warm water for a few minutes, or leave it at room temperature. Do not microwave the oil directly in a sealed container. Repeated melting and re-solidification does not degrade the oil, provided the container is kept sealed and moisture-free.

---

### 4. How should I store the oil to maximise its shelf life?

Store in a cool, dark location away from direct sunlight and heat sources. A kitchen cupboard away from the stove is ideal. Refrigeration is not necessary but is acceptable — it will simply keep the oil in solid form.

Key storage rules:
- Always close the lid tightly after use to prevent moisture contamination and oxidation.
- Use a clean, dry spoon if scooping solid oil — never introduce water into the bottle.
- Avoid storing near strong-smelling substances, as coconut oil can absorb ambient odours over time.
- Opened oil is best used within **12–18 months**. Unopened, it keeps for **18–24 months** from the production date.

Discard the oil if it develops a rancid, bitter, or soapy smell — these are signs of oxidation.

### 5. Can I cook with this oil? What is its smoke point?

Yes, cold pressed coconut oil is suitable for cooking at low to medium heat. Its smoke point is approximately **175–200°C**, which is adequate for:

- Sautéing and stir-frying at medium heat
- Baking (as a butter or ghee substitute)
- Light shallow frying
- Tadka / tempering at moderate temperatures
- Adding to warm dishes, porridge, or smoothies without cooking

It is **not ideal for high-heat deep frying** (which requires ~200°C+). For high-heat applications, refined coconut oil or mustard oil is more appropriate. Using any oil above its smoke point generates harmful free radicals and degradation compounds regardless of how nutritious the base oil is.

The natural aroma of cold pressed coconut oil also dissipates substantially during high-heat cooking, which reduces the functional difference between cold pressed and refined oil when used at very high temperatures.

### 6. Can I use this oil on my hair and skin?

Yes. Cold pressed coconut oil is widely used for topical applications and performs well for:

- **Hair:** Scalp massage, pre-wash oil treatment, conditioning dry or damaged hair ends. Coconut oil has a relatively low molecular weight and can partially penetrate the hair shaft, reducing protein loss.
- **Skin:** Body moisturiser, dry skin relief, lip care, gentle makeup remover, and massage oil.
- **Baby skin:** Traditional use on infant skin is common and generally considered safe on intact skin. Avoid the eye area and do not apply to broken or infected skin without medical guidance.

If you are using it topically for the first time, do a patch test on your inner forearm and wait 24 hours before widespread application. Rare cases of contact dermatitis have been reported even in people without a systemic coconut allergy.

### 7. Is this oil suitable for oil pulling?

Yes, cold pressed virgin coconut oil is the most commonly recommended oil for oil pulling due to its lauric acid content, natural antimicrobial properties, and pleasant mild flavour.

Standard practice: swish 1 tablespoon (15mL) in your mouth for 10–20 minutes, then spit it out — do not swallow after oil pulling, as the oil collects oral bacteria during the process. Spit into a bin rather than the sink to avoid pipes clogging as the oil re-solidifies.

Oil pulling is a traditional practice. While some studies suggest modest benefits for oral hygiene, it is not a substitute for regular brushing, flossing, or professional dental care.

- **Cold pressed virgin coconut oil** requires fresh, mature coconuts (more expensive raw material), careful mechanical extraction at controlled temperatures, lower yield per coconut compared to solvent extraction, and more careful quality management throughout.

### 10. Does this oil have any certifications?

Certification practices for locally produced cold pressed coconut oil in Bangladesh are not yet standardised across the industry. BSTI (Bangladesh Standards and Testing Institution) certification and food safety registration are the primary relevant domestic frameworks.

Ask the supplier for:
- A current batch Certificate of Analysis (COA) from an accredited laboratory
- Any available BSTI registration documentation
- Production and expiry date on the packaging

This product does not currently carry international organic certifications (e.g. USDA Organic, EU Organic). If international certification is a requirement for your use case, imported certified brands may be more suitable — though they come at a higher price and with longer supply chains.

### 11. I have high cholesterol. Should I be using coconut oil?

This is a question you should discuss with your doctor, not rely on product content to answer. Here is the factual context:

Coconut oil is very high in saturated fat (~82–90% of total fat), primarily lauric acid. Current mainstream cardiovascular guidelines (WHO, American Heart Association) recommend limiting saturated fat intake, particularly for individuals with elevated LDL cholesterol or established cardiovascular disease.

The metabolic effects of lauric acid specifically are the subject of ongoing scientific debate — some research suggests it raises both LDL and HDL cholesterol, with the net cardiovascular impact being unclear. The scientific consensus has not resolved this debate definitively as of 2026.

Using small amounts of cold pressed coconut oil in cooking as one part of a varied diet is different from consuming it in large supplemental doses. If you have hyperlipidaemia or cardiovascular disease, consult your physician or cardiologist before making coconut oil a regular part of your diet.

### 12. Can I give this to my child or use it for baby massage?

For children over 2 years, small culinary amounts are generally considered safe as part of a balanced diet. Introduce gradually and observe for any signs of digestive sensitivity.

For infant skin and hair massage, traditional use of coconut oil is widespread in Bangladesh and South Asia. Cold pressed coconut oil is generally considered safe for topical use on intact infant skin. Use small amounts, avoid the eye area, and do not apply to broken, inflamed, or infected skin.

For dietary introduction to infants under 12 months, consult your paediatrician. There is no specific contraindication (as exists with honey, for example), but any new food for very young infants should be introduced under medical guidance.

### 15. The oil smells slightly different from a previous batch. Is something wrong?

Minor variation in aroma intensity and character between batches is normal for an unrefined, single-ingredient natural product. Factors that cause natural variation include the origin region of the coconuts, seasonal differences, coconut maturity at harvest, and slight differences in pressing conditions.

What you should be concerned about:
- A **rancid, bitter, soapy, or chemical smell** — this indicates oxidation or adulteration and the oil should not be used.
- A **complete absence of coconut scent** — may suggest the batch is refined rather than cold pressed.
- A **strong artificial or perfume-like coconut scent** — may indicate added artificial fragrance to a refined base.

A mildly different but still recognisably fresh coconut aroma between batches is not a quality concern. If you are uncertain about a specific batch, contact the supplier with the production date and batch information on the label.
