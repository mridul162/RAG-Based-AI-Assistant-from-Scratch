## Allergen Information

<section id="allergens">

**Primary allergen: Tree nut (coconut)**

Coconut is classified as a tree nut by some regulatory bodies (including the US FDA), though it is botanically a fruit/drupe. This classification has practical implications for allergy sufferers.

<table>

| Allergen Concern | Risk Level | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Coconut allergy** | Low prevalence, but real | True coconut allergy exists and can range from mild (contact dermatitis, oral allergy syndrome) to severe (anaphylaxis). Individuals with diagnosed coconut allergy must avoid this product entirely. |
| **Tree nut cross-reactivity** | Low to moderate | Some individuals allergic to tree nuts (walnut, cashew, etc.) may react to coconut. Cross-reactivity is not universal — consult an allergist before use. |
| **Latex-fruit syndrome** | Low | Coconut has been occasionally reported as a secondary trigger in latex-allergic individuals. Not well-established but worth noting for latex-allergic customers. |
| **Contact dermatitis (topical use)** | Low | Rare cases of skin irritation or contact dermatitis with topical application have been reported, even without systemic coconut allergy. Patch test recommended for first-time topical users. |

**Contains:** Coconut (tree nut).  
**Does not contain:** Gluten, dairy, soy, peanut, sesame, or mustard.  
**Cross-contamination:** Depends on processing facility. Buyers with severe multi-allergen sensitivities should confirm facility practices with the supplier directly.

> ⚠️ If a customer reports a known coconut or tree nut allergy, advise them to consult their allergist before purchasing.

</section>

## Who Should Avoid or Exercise Caution

<section id="contraindications">

**Do not use / use only under medical supervision:**

**1. Individuals with confirmed coconut allergy**  
Any topical or dietary use should be avoided. Even refined coconut oil may retain trace allergenic proteins.

**2. Individuals with hyperlipidaemia or established cardiovascular disease**  
Cold pressed coconut oil is very high in saturated fat (~82–90% of total fat content, primarily lauric acid). While the metabolic effects of lauric acid are debated in the literature, mainstream cardiovascular health guidelines (WHO, American Heart Association) recommend limiting saturated fat intake. People with high LDL cholesterol, familial hypercholesterolaemia, or existing heart disease should use coconut oil only after consulting their cardiologist or physician.

**3. Individuals on low-fat therapeutic diets**  
Patients prescribed low-fat diets for pancreatitis, gallbladder disease, or fat malabsorption disorders (e.g., short bowel syndrome, Crohn's disease with fat malabsorption) should consult their doctor before adding any oil, including coconut oil.

**4. Infants under 12 months (dietary use)**  
No specific contraindication equivalent to honey exists for coconut oil, but introducing any new fat source to infants should be guided by a paediatrician. Topical use on infant skin is generally considered safe and is a traditional practice, but use only on intact skin and avoid eye area.

**5. Individuals managing obesity**  
Coconut oil is calorically dense (~900 kcal per 100mL). Routine high-volume consumption can contribute meaningfully to caloric surplus. Consumption should be factored into total daily caloric intake.

**6. People with coconut protein sensitivity (topical use)**  
Some skincare and cosmetic products use hydrolysed coconut protein, which behaves differently from whole coconut oil. However, individuals who experience reactions to coconut-derived cosmetics should patch test before regular topical application of this oil.

**Use with caution:**

- Elderly individuals on multiple medications (see drug interactions below)
- Pregnant or breastfeeding women — culinary use in normal food amounts is considered safe; high supplemental doses have insufficient safety data
- Individuals with existing liver conditions — the liver primarily metabolises MCTs; consult a physician for supplemental (non-culinary) use

## Drug & Condition Interactions

<section id="interactions">

> **Disclaimer:** The interactions below are based on available evidence as of 2026. This is not an exhaustive list. Customers on prescription medications should consult their pharmacist or physician before using coconut oil as a dietary supplement (i.e., in amounts significantly above normal culinary use).

<table>

| Drug / Condition | Nature of Interaction | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| **Lipid-lowering drugs (statins, fibrates)** | Coconut oil's high saturated fat content may partially counteract the LDL-lowering effect of statins (e.g., atorvastatin, rosuvastatin) if consumed in large amounts. Effect at normal culinary doses is likely minimal. | Use in normal culinary amounts is probably acceptable; avoid high supplemental doses without physician guidance. |
| **Anticoagulants / blood thinners (warfarin, heparin)** | Some early research suggests MCTs may have minor effects on platelet aggregation. Evidence is not conclusive. | Monitor INR if introducing large amounts of coconut oil while on warfarin; inform prescribing physician. |
| **Diabetes medications (insulin, metformin, sulfonylureas)** | High saturated fat diets are associated with insulin resistance over time. Short-term MCT use has been studied with mixed results on glycaemic control. | Diabetic patients may use culinary amounts; supplemental/high-dose use should be discussed with their diabetes care team. |
| **Immunosuppressants (post-transplant, autoimmune)** | Lauric acid has documented antimicrobial and immune-modulating properties. Theoretical interaction with immunosuppressant efficacy exists but is not clinically established. | Advise patients on immunosuppressants to consult their specialist before supplemental use. |
| **Gallbladder disease / post-cholecystectomy** | All dietary fats, including coconut oil, stimulate bile secretion. Active gallstones or post-surgical sensitivity may lead to discomfort. | Avoid high-dose use during gallbladder attacks; normal culinary use may be tolerated post-cholecystectomy but individual tolerance varies. |
| **Thyroid conditions (hypothyroidism)** | Coconut oil is frequently marketed as beneficial for thyroid function — this claim lacks robust clinical evidence. No established harmful drug interaction with levothyroxine or other thyroid medications is documented, but unverified health claims should not drive therapeutic decisions. | Normal culinary use is likely safe; do not substitute for prescribed thyroid medication or alter medication timing based on coconut oil use. |

</section>

## Safe Usage Limits

<section id="safe-limits">

**Dietary (internal) use:**

There is no universally agreed maximum safe daily dose for coconut oil. The following guidance is based on current nutritional and clinical evidence:

<table>

| Context | Suggested Limit | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **General healthy adult (culinary use)** | 1–2 tablespoons (15–30mL) per day as part of a balanced diet | Consistent with amounts used in most positive MCT research; keeps daily saturated fat within manageable range |
| **Cardiovascular risk individuals** | Minimise or avoid as primary cooking fat; use sparingly as a flavour component | AHA / WHO saturated fat guidance: <10% of total daily calories from saturated fat |
| **Children (>2 years)** | Small culinary amounts only — not recommended as a supplement | No established paediatric supplemental dose |
| **As a supplement (MCT focus)** | Start with 1 tsp (5mL), increase slowly to max ~2 tbsp (30mL) per day | Higher doses cause gastrointestinal discomfort (nausea, cramping, loose stools) in many individuals |

**Introducing coconut oil for the first time (dietary):**  
Start with small amounts (1 teaspoon per day) and increase gradually over 1–2 weeks. Rapid introduction of significant MCT quantities commonly causes nausea, diarrhoea, or stomach cramping.

**Topical use:**

- No established maximum for topical application on intact adult skin.
- **Patch test:** Apply a small amount to the inner forearm, leave for 24 hours before first widespread use.
- Avoid application near eyes or on broken, infected, or acutely inflamed skin without medical guidance.
- For infant scalp or skin use: small amounts only; avoid occluding large skin areas under tight wrapping, which can impair temperature regulation.

**Oil pulling (oral use):**

- Standard practice: 1 tablespoon (15mL) swished for 10–20 minutes, then spat out — do not swallow after oil pulling.
- Not a substitute for standard dental hygiene or prescribed dental treatment.
- No established maximum frequency; once daily is the commonly cited practice.

**Storage safety:**

- Store in a cool, dark place away from direct sunlight.
- Recommended use within **12–18 months** of production date once opened.
- Do not use if the oil smells rancid, bitter, or off — oxidised oil should be discarded.
- Keep container tightly sealed to prevent moisture contamination, which accelerates rancidity.

</section>
