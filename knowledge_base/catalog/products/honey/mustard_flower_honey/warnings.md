## Allergen Information

<section id="allergens">

Mustard flower honey carries two distinct allergen considerations: one from honey itself, and one specific to its floral source.

### Honey Allergens (General)
Raw honey may contain trace amounts of:
- **Bee proteins** (including bee venom proteins) — a potential trigger for individuals with bee/wasp venom allergies
- **Propolis** — a resinous bee product present in raw honey at low levels; some individuals are sensitive to it
- **Royal jelly** — may be present in trace amounts; known to cause allergic reactions in sensitive individuals

### Mustard-Specific Allergen (Important)
Mustard (*Brassica* spp.) is a **recognized food allergen** listed under major allergen frameworks in several countries. Mustard flower honey contains mustard pollen and potentially trace floral compounds from mustard blossoms.

**Individuals with a known mustard or Brassica allergy should treat this product with caution and consult a doctor before consuming it.**

This is a meaningful distinction from generic multifloral honey and should be communicated clearly to buyers who ask about allergens.

| Allergen | Present | Level | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bee proteins | Yes | Trace | Risk for bee venom-allergic individuals |
| Propolis | Yes | Trace | Risk for propolis-sensitive individuals |
| Mustard / Brassica pollen | Yes | Moderate | Risk for mustard-allergic individuals |
| Gluten | No | None | Honey is naturally gluten-free |
| Dairy | No | None | — |

> This product is produced in an apiary environment. Cross-contamination with other floral pollens is inherent to natural honey and cannot be fully excluded.

## Who Should Avoid

<section id="contraindications">

### ⚠️ Infants Under 12 Months — Do Not Use
**Honey of any type must not be given to children under 12 months of age.** Raw honey may contain spores of *Clostridium botulinum*, a bacterium harmless to adults and older children but capable of germinating in an infant's immature digestive system and producing botulinum toxin, causing **infant botulism** — a serious and potentially fatal illness.

This applies regardless of the honey's purity, origin, or how it is administered (directly, in food, or on a pacifier).

**There are no exceptions to this restriction.**

### Individuals with Mustard or Brassica Allergy
As noted above, mustard pollen is present in this honey. Anyone who reacts to mustard seeds, mustard oil, or other Brassica vegetables (e.g. cabbage, broccoli, cauliflower) in an allergic (not intolerance-based) way should avoid this product or seek medical clearance first.

### Individuals with Diagnosed Bee Venom Allergy (Severe)
Those with a documented severe allergy to bee stings (anaphylaxis history) should exercise caution with raw honey products and consult an allergist before use.

### People with Uncontrolled Diabetes
Honey is a high-glycemic food. Mustard flower honey is predominantly glucose and fructose. While it is a natural sugar source, it will raise blood glucose levels. People with uncontrolled or poorly managed diabetes should not use honey as a free food. Those with well-managed diabetes may consume small amounts under dietary guidance.

### People with Fructose Malabsorption
Honey contains significant fructose. Those with diagnosed fructose malabsorption may experience bloating, cramping, or diarrhea.

## Drug / Condition Interactions

<section id="interactions">

| Condition / Drug | Interaction | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| **Diabetes medications** (insulin, metformin, sulfonylureas) | Honey raises blood glucose and may affect medication dosing requirements | Use only under dietary supervision; monitor blood glucose if consuming regularly |
| **Warfarin / blood thinners** | Large amounts of honey may have a mild anticoagulant effect; evidence is limited but noted | Keep consumption moderate and consistent; inform prescribing doctor |
| **Immunosuppressed patients** (chemotherapy, organ transplant, HIV) | Raw honey carries a low but non-zero microbial load; pasteurized honey is generally recommended for this group | Consult treating physician before use; this product is raw/natural, not pasteurized |
| **Pollen allergy / hay fever** | Mustard pollen in the honey may trigger or worsen pollen allergy symptoms in sensitive individuals during allergy season | Start with a very small amount to test tolerance; discontinue if symptoms worsen |
| **Gastrointestinal conditions** (IBS, SIBO) | Honey's fructose and fermentable sugars may aggravate symptoms in some GI conditions | Use in small amounts; monitor tolerance |

> This table covers the most clinically relevant interactions. It is not exhaustive. Customers taking prescribed medications for any chronic condition should check with their doctor or pharmacist if unsure.

## Safe Usage Limits

<section id="safe-usage">

### General Healthy Adults
- A commonly referenced moderate daily intake of honey for adults is **1–2 tablespoons (approximately 20–40g) per day** as part of a normal diet.
- There is no evidence that mustard flower honey requires a lower limit than other honeys when consumed in moderation.
- Consuming honey in excess of this regularly contributes meaningfully to daily sugar intake and is not recommended for weight management or dental health.

### Children (1 Year and Older)
- Small amounts (½–1 teaspoon) are generally considered safe for children over 12 months.
- Honey should not be a primary sweetener in a child's regular diet.
- **Under 12 months: strictly avoid** (see above).

### Heating
- Do not mix into boiling water or cook at high temperatures. Excessive heat (above ~60°C) degrades beneficial enzymes and antioxidants. Add to warm — not boiling — water or food.
- Avoid storing near a heat source or in direct sunlight.

### Storage
- Store in a sealed container at room temperature, away from moisture.
- Do not refrigerate — cold accelerates crystallization and makes the honey harder to use.
- Crystallized honey is safe to eat as-is or can be gently liquefied in a warm water bath (below 40°C).
- No expiry in the traditional sense, but best consumed within **18–24 months** of harvest for optimal flavor and enzyme activity.

### Topical Use
- When used on skin or minor wounds, apply a thin layer only. Discontinue if redness, swelling, or irritation develops — this may indicate a propolis or pollen sensitivity.
