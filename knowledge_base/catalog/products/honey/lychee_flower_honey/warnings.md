## Allergen Information

<section id="allergens">

Honey is a natural product and may contain or trigger the following allergens:

| Allergen | Details |
|---|---|
| **Pollen** | Lychee flower honey contains trace amounts of lychee (*Litchi chinensis*) pollen. Individuals with pollen allergies — particularly to tree pollens — may react. |
| **Bee-derived proteins** | Honey may contain trace bee proteins (from saliva, enzymes). Those with known bee or venom allergies should exercise caution. |
| **Royal jelly / propolis** | Natural honey may contain trace amounts of propolis or royal jelly, which are known contact allergens in sensitive individuals. |
| **Cross-reactive fruits** | People with latex-fruit syndrome or lychee fruit allergy may experience cross-reactivity with lychee pollen residues in the honey. |

**If you experience itching, swelling, difficulty breathing, or any signs of allergic reaction after consuming honey, discontinue use immediately and seek medical attention.**

</section>

## Who Should Avoid

<section id="contraindications">

The following groups should **not consume** this product or should consult a doctor before use:

### ⛔ Must Avoid

- **Infants under 12 months (1 year):** All honey — including natural, raw, and processed honey — poses a **serious risk of infant botulism**. *Clostridium botulinum* spores can survive in honey and produce toxins in an infant's immature digestive system, potentially causing paralysis or death. **Never feed honey to babies under 1 year of age under any circumstances.**

- **Individuals with confirmed bee product or pollen allergies:** Risk of allergic reaction, including anaphylaxis.

### ⚠️ Should Consult a Doctor First

- **People with diabetes (Type 1 or Type 2):** Honey is high in natural sugars (primarily fructose and glucose). It can raise blood glucose levels. Diabetics should only consume honey under medical supervision and within a tracked dietary plan.

- **People with fructose malabsorption or hereditary fructose intolerance:** Honey is high in fructose and may cause significant gastrointestinal distress in these individuals.

- **Immunocompromised individuals** (e.g. those undergoing chemotherapy, organ transplant recipients, people with HIV/AIDS): Raw honey may contain naturally occurring bacteria or spores that a weakened immune system cannot adequately fight off.

- **Pregnant and breastfeeding women:** Honey is generally considered safe for healthy adults during pregnancy. However, immunocompromised pregnant women or those with gestational diabetes should consult their physician.

- **People with irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) or following a low-FODMAP diet:** Fructose and oligosaccharides in honey may trigger IBS symptoms.

## Drug & Condition Interactions

<section id="interactions">

| Drug / Condition | Interaction | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| **Warfarin / blood thinners** | Honey may have mild anticoagulant properties; high consumption could potentially influence INR levels. | Avoid consuming large or irregular amounts; inform your physician. |
| **Insulin / oral hypoglycaemics** | Honey raises blood glucose; it can interfere with dosing requirements for diabetic medications. | Monitor blood glucose carefully. Do not use as a sugar substitute without medical advice. |
| **Antibiotics** | Honey has natural antimicrobial properties. While this does not typically cause a direct drug interaction, it should not be used as a substitute for prescribed antibiotic treatment. | Continue prescribed antibiotics as directed; do not replace with honey. |
| **Diabetes (uncontrolled)** | The high glycaemic load of honey can destabilise blood sugar control. | Avoid or use strictly under physician guidance. |
| **Phenylketonuria (PKU)** | Honey itself does not contain phenylalanine and is not a direct concern, but this flag is maintained for completeness when combined with other dietary restrictions. | No specific restriction; general dietary management applies. |

> **Note:** This list covers the most commonly documented interactions. Always inform your healthcare provider about all foods and supplements you are consuming, especially if you are on regular medication.

</section>

## Safe Usage Limits

<section id="safe-usage">

Honey is a natural food, not a medicine. The following guidance reflects general health recommendations for healthy adults:

| Group | Recommended Limit |
|---|---|
| **Healthy adults** | Up to **1–2 tablespoons (20–40g) per day** as part of a balanced diet is generally considered safe. |
| **Children (1–12 years)** | Small amounts only (1 teaspoon / ~5–10g per day). Not a nutritional necessity; limit added sugar intake per paediatric dietary guidelines. |
| **Adolescents (13–17 years)** | Up to 1 tablespoon (15–20g) per day, within total daily sugar intake limits. |
| **Diabetics / pre-diabetics** | Only as advised by a physician. Even small amounts can affect blood sugar. |
| **Infants under 12 months** | **Zero. Strictly prohibited.** |

### Additional Notes

- Honey is a source of **natural sugars and calories** (~300–320 kcal per 100g). Excessive consumption may contribute to weight gain, tooth decay, and elevated triglycerides.
- **Do not heat honey at very high temperatures** (above ~40°C / 104°F) for consumption — it degrades beneficial enzymes and may increase hydroxymethylfurfural (HMF) content.
- Store in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight. Keep the lid tightly closed to prevent moisture absorption and fermentation.
- Natural honey may **crystallise** over time. This is normal and does not indicate spoilage. Gently warm the jar in lukewarm water to re-liquefy.
- This product is **seasonal and raw/natural**. Composition (colour, aroma, viscosity) may vary slightly between batches depending on the harvest season.

</section>
