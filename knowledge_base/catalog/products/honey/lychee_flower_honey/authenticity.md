## Visual Indicators

<section id="visual">

Lychee flower honey has one of the most distinctive appearances of any Bangladeshi single-origin honey. Its light colour is its most immediately recognisable feature and the first thing to check.

<table>

| Attribute | Genuine Lychee Flower Honey | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| **Colour** | Very light — pale gold to water-white; one of the lightest honeys available in Bangladesh | Dark amber, brown, or yellowish-green tones |
| **Transparency** | Clear to slightly hazy when fresh and liquid | Cloudy with visible suspended particles (not pollen — large debris) |
| **Texture** | Light to medium body; pours in a thin, smooth ribbon at room temperature | Watery and runny like diluted syrup, or unnaturally thick and gel-like |
| **Crystallisation** | Crystallises into a fine, soft, creamy white mass over weeks to months | Never crystallises after months of storage; or crystallises in hard, gritty, uneven chunks |
| **Foam/Bubbles** | Small, natural fine bubbles may be present near the surface | Excessive froth or no bubbles whatsoever in a fresh jar |
| **Sediment** | Fine natural pollen sediment at the bottom is normal and expected in raw honey | Completely sediment-free (over-filtered) or visible foreign matter |

</table>

### Colour Is the Primary Visual Marker

Genuine lychee flower honey is notably pale — sometimes almost translucent. This is its most reliable visual identifier. If someone sells you a honey labelled as lychee that is amber or dark in colour, it is almost certainly not single-origin lychee honey. It may be multifloral, mustard-blended, or a different variety entirely.

### Crystallisation as an Authenticity Signal

Authentic lychee honey will crystallise naturally at room temperature over time, typically forming a soft, white, creamy texture — similar in consistency to soft butter or fine-grain yoghurt. This is not a defect. Honey that remains completely liquid for many months without any sign of crystallisation is a strong indicator that glucose syrup or other adulterants have been added to inhibit this natural process.

**Quick refrigerator test:** Place a small amount in the refrigerator (4–8°C) for 48 hours. Genuine honey will show visible cloudiness or partial crystallisation beginning to form.

## Taste & Aroma Indicators

<section id="sensory">

The sensory profile of lychee flower honey is delicate and distinctive. It is one of the more easily identified single-origin honeys by taste alone — once you have tried genuine lychee honey, substitutes are noticeable.

### Aroma

- **Expected:** Light, floral, and faintly fruity — a soft scent reminiscent of lychee fruit, rose water, and fresh blossoms; gentle rather than intense
- **Intensity:** Subtle; it does not hit you when you open the jar the way mustard or black seed honey does
- **Red Flag:** Neutral, plain sweet smell with no floral note; artificial rose or fruit fragrance that smells more like perfume than flower; or any caramel, burnt sugar, or molasses undertone

### Taste

- **Initial flavour:** Clean, light sweetness — not heavy or cloying
- **Mid-palate:** Distinct floral note; a faint fruity character reminiscent of lychee or rose
- **Finish:** Soft and clean; fades gently without a sharp, bitter, or acidic aftertaste
- **Texture on tongue:** Smooth, light-bodied; coats the mouth gently
- **Red Flag:** Heavy, thick sweetness with no floral dimension; sharp chemical or artificial fruit taste; bitterness or spice note (suggests blending with black seed or other honey); astringent aftertaste

### Practical Sensory Test

1. Take ½ teaspoon at room temperature on an empty or neutral palate.
2. Let it rest on your tongue for 10–15 seconds before swallowing.
3. Genuine lychee honey will show a clean floral sweetness — simple, light, and pleasant with no harsh edges.
4. The aftertaste should fade within 30–45 seconds, leaving no strong residue.

The key descriptor is **lightness**. If the honey feels heavy, tastes complex, or has any bitter or spicy dimension, it is not pure lychee flower honey.

</section>

## Lab / Certification Checks

<section id="certification">

For buyers requiring documented assurance — particularly institutional buyers, resellers, or those purchasing in volume — the following checks apply to honey sourced from Bangladesh.

### Documents to Request from the Seller

<table>

| Document / Test | What It Confirms | Notes for Bangladesh Context |
|---|---|---|
| **Pollen analysis (Melissopalynology)** | Confirms *Litchi chinensis* pollen as dominant source; verifies floral origin | The most reliable single test for confirming lychee flower honey; should show lychee pollen as ≥45% of total pollen count for monofloral classification |
| **Moisture content test** | Should be ≤20% per Codex Alimentarius standard | Above 20% indicates premature harvesting or water addition; lychee honey harvested correctly typically falls between 17–19% |
| **HMF (Hydroxymethylfurfural) test** | Should be <40 mg/kg (Codex standard) | High HMF indicates overheating or old/adulterated product |
| **Sucrose content test** | Should be <5% per Codex standard | Elevated sucrose indicates sugar syrup feeding of bees or direct adulteration |
| **Diastase activity** | Should be ≥8 on the Schade scale | Low activity indicates excessive heat treatment or artificial honey |
| **BSTI certification** | Bangladesh Standards and Testing Institution compliance | Confirms the product meets national food safety standards; not all retail honey carries this |

</table>

### Realistic Expectations for Retail Purchases

Most retail-scale buyers (250g–1kg) will not receive lab reports as standard. This is normal for the Bangladeshi honey market at the retail level and does not by itself indicate fraud. For quantities above 5kg, for resale, or for institutional procurement, requesting a moisture content test result and any available BSTI documentation is reasonable.

At the retail level, sensory and visual checks (above) remain the most practical tools available to buyers.

</section>

## Common Fakes to Watch For

<section id="fakes">

Lychee flower honey is a premium seasonal product with limited annual availability, making it a target for adulteration and mislabelling. The light colour is both its most recognisable feature and, paradoxically, one of the easier characteristics to replicate through adulteration.

### Adulteration Methods

<table>

| Method | How It Is Done | How to Detect |
|---|---|---|
| **Sugar syrup blending** | Plain sucrose syrup, glucose syrup, or high-fructose corn syrup mixed into real honey to increase volume | Watery texture; no crystallisation after months; overly simple sweetness with no floral dimension; sucrose test will show elevated levels |
| **Mislabelled light multifloral honey** | Any pale-coloured wildflower honey sold as lychee honey | Lacks the specific lychee-floral aroma and taste note; pollen test will show mixed or absent lychee pollen |
| **Off-season honey relabelled as lychee** | Honey harvested from other seasons sold under the lychee label after the seasonal stock runs out | Correct channel: ask when the honey was harvested; lychee honey in Bangladesh is harvested primarily April–June |
| **Bee sugar-feeding** | Bees fed sugar solution during or after lychee season; collected "honey" is essentially processed syrup | Very thin; no complex aroma; elevated sucrose; does not crystallise naturally |
| **Artificial fragrance addition** | Rose water, lychee flavouring, or food-grade floral essence added to plain honey to simulate lychee aroma | Aroma is perfume-like and sharp rather than soft and natural; the scent does not change or mellow when the honey is warmed slightly |
| **Blending with mustard honey** | Cheap, widely available mustard honey blended with a small amount of genuine lychee honey, sold as pure lychee | Colour will be noticeably more yellow or golden; slight pungency in the aroma; mustard honey pollen will appear in analysis |

</table>

### Specific Red Flags for This Product

- **Amber or golden colour labelled as lychee honey** — genuine lychee honey is pale, not amber. Any meaningfully dark-coloured honey sold as lychee is not single-origin lychee honey.
- **Available year-round in unlimited quantities from the same seller** — lychee honey has a narrow seasonal harvest window (approximately April–June in Bangladesh). A seller with unlimited stock in November or January warrants scrutiny.
- **Price significantly below market rate** — genuine single-origin lychee honey at ৳600/kg reflects the seasonal, limited-harvest nature of the product. Deep discounts from that level are a risk signal.
- **Strong perfume-like floral smell** — authentic lychee honey smells softly floral. If the aroma is intense, sharp, or smells like lychee candy rather than lychee blossom, artificial fragrance is likely present.
- **Honey that never shows any crystallisation** — lychee honey crystallises. If a jar remains fully liquid for 6+ months at room temperature, adulteration with invert sugar syrup is highly probable.

### Simple Home Verification Tests

**Water dispersion test:**
Drop ½ teaspoon of honey into a glass of still water at room temperature and do not stir.
- Genuine honey sinks slowly and forms a small lump or loose thread at the bottom, dissolving only gradually.
- Adulterated honey with syrup dissolves and disperses quickly into the water.

**Thumb test:**
Place a small drop on your thumb. Genuine honey stays in place and spreads very slowly. Thin, watery honey that runs off immediately suggests high moisture content or syrup dilution.

*These home tests are indicative only. They can detect obvious adulteration but will not identify sophisticated blending. Pollen analysis remains the definitive test for confirming lychee floral origin.*

</section>
