## ⚠️ Critical Warning — Infants

> **Do NOT give any honey — including this product — to children under 12 months of age.**
>
> Raw honey may contain spores of *Clostridium botulinum*. In infants, these spores can germinate in the immature digestive tract and produce botulinum toxin, causing **infant botulism** — a potentially fatal condition. This risk applies to ALL raw honey regardless of source or purity. Pasteurization does not fully eliminate this risk either. The restriction is absolute: **no honey for babies under 1 year**.

## Allergen Information

<section id="allergens">

This product contains or may contain the following allergens:

| Allergen | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Bee pollen** | Present naturally in raw comb honey. A known and significant allergen. Can trigger reactions ranging from mild oral allergy syndrome to severe anaphylaxis in sensitized individuals. |
| **Propolis** | Present in trace amounts. Cross-reacts with balsam of Peru, certain resins, and some topical products. Can cause allergic contact reactions. |
| **Royal jelly** | May be present in trace amounts in wild comb honey. Associated with asthma, anaphylaxis, and dermatitis in sensitive individuals, particularly those with existing asthma or atopy. |
| **Beeswax** | The natural comb is consumed alongside the honey. Beeswax allergy is rare but documented. |
| **Floral allergens** | Pollen from Sundarbans mangrove and forest flora is carried in the honey. Cross-reactivity with specific plant allergies is possible. |

**If you have a known allergy to bee products, bee stings, pollen, or any component listed above, consult a physician before consuming this product.**

</section>

## Who Should Avoid

<section id="contraindications">

### Absolute — Do Not Consume

- **Infants under 12 months:** Risk of infant botulism (see critical warning above).
- **Individuals with confirmed bee product allergy:** Includes allergy to bee pollen, propolis, royal jelly, or beeswax.
- **Individuals with known anaphylaxis to honey:** Do not consume under any circumstances without medical clearance.

---

### Exercise Caution — Consult a Doctor First

- **Children aged 1–3 years:** Honey is generally considered safe after 12 months, but raw wild honey should be introduced cautiously and in small amounts due to higher microbial content compared to pasteurized honey.
- **Pregnant women:** Raw honey carries a small risk of exposure to environmental contaminants and unpasteurized microbial content. Medical guidance varies; consult your OB/GYN.
- **Breastfeeding mothers:** Generally considered low-risk, but consult a physician if in doubt.
- **Immunocompromised individuals:** Including those undergoing chemotherapy, on long-term immunosuppressants, or living with HIV/AIDS. Raw honey is not sterile and may carry microbial or fungal spores that a healthy immune system handles easily but a compromised one may not.
- **Individuals with asthma or atopic conditions:** Royal jelly and bee pollen in raw honey are associated with asthma exacerbation and allergic reactions in atopic individuals.
- **Diabetics:** Honey is high in natural sugars (primarily fructose and glucose) and will raise blood glucose. It is not a safe sugar substitute for people managing diabetes. Consume only under medical advice and within a controlled dietary plan.
- **Individuals on low-fructose or low-FODMAP diets:** Honey is high in fructose and is a high-FODMAP food. May trigger symptoms in people with fructose malabsorption or IBS.
- **People with pollen food allergy syndrome (PFAS/OAS):** Cross-reactive pollen in raw honey may trigger oral allergy symptoms (itching or tingling in the mouth/throat).

## Drug & Condition Interactions

<section id="interactions">

| Drug / Condition | Interaction | Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| **Warfarin (Coumadin) and other anticoagulants** | Honey, particularly in large amounts, may have mild antiplatelet effects and could theoretically interact with blood thinners. | Do not consume in large quantities; inform your doctor if you use anticoagulants regularly. |
| **Antidiabetic medications (insulin, metformin, etc.)** | Honey raises blood glucose and may counteract glucose-lowering medications or cause unpredictable glycemic swings. | Diabetics should avoid or consume only under direct medical supervision with blood glucose monitoring. |
| **Antibiotics** | No established direct interaction, but honey has natural antimicrobial properties (hydrogen peroxide, propolis). No clinically significant drug interaction documented at food consumption levels. | Generally safe alongside antibiotics at normal dietary amounts. |
| **MAO inhibitors (MAOIs)** | No direct interaction documented with honey specifically. Tyramine content in fermented or over-aged honey is a theoretical concern. | Avoid if honey shows signs of fermentation (bubbling, sour smell, alcohol taste). |
| **Allergy immunotherapy (desensitization)** | Consuming bee pollen in raw honey while undergoing pollen immunotherapy may interfere with or complicate the therapy. | Consult your allergist before consuming raw honey during immunotherapy. |
| **Compromised gut flora (post-antibiotic, IBD, Crohn's)** | Raw honey contains wild yeasts and microorganisms. These are harmless to a healthy gut but may be disruptive when gut flora is disturbed. | Exercise caution after a course of antibiotics or during IBD flare-ups. |

</section>

## Safe Usage Limits

<section id="safe-limits">

There is no universally established medical upper limit for honey consumption in healthy adults, but the following guidance is based on nutritional science and general health recommendations:

**Healthy adults:**
- A commonly cited moderate intake is **1–2 tablespoons (approximately 20–40g) per day** as part of a balanced diet.
- At this level, honey contributes roughly 60–120 kcal and 15–30g of sugar per day.

**Key considerations:**

- **This is raw, unpasteurized honey.** It retains natural enzymes, pollen, and wild microorganisms. These are generally beneficial, but consuming very large quantities daily of any raw product is not recommended.
- **Sugar load:** Even natural sugar is sugar. Overconsumption contributes to caloric surplus, dental caries, and blood glucose spikes. The WHO recommends keeping free sugar intake below 10% of total daily energy intake (about 50g/day for an average adult — honey counts toward this).
- **Honey as medicine:** This product is sold as a food, not a medicine. Medicinal claims (immunity, wound healing, etc.) are traditional and not a substitute for professional medical treatment.
- **Do not heat excessively:** Heating above 40°C (104°F) destroys the natural enzymes, antioxidants, and antimicrobial compounds that make raw honey valuable. Do not dissolve in boiling water or cook into high-heat recipes if retaining raw properties is important to you.

</section>

## Storage Safety Note

Improperly stored raw honey can ferment if moisture content is high or the jar is left open. **Fermented honey should not be consumed** — discard if you notice:
- Active bubbling or fizzing inside a sealed jar
- Sour, alcoholic, or "off" smell
- Visible mold on the comb or honey surface

Store in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight. Keep tightly sealed after opening.
