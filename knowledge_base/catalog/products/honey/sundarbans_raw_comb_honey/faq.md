### 1. What exactly is comb honey, and how is it different from regular honey?

Comb honey is honey that is sold inside the natural beeswax comb that the bees built themselves. Instead of extracting the honey and discarding the comb, the entire structure — wax cells, honey, pollen, propolis, and all — is packaged together.

Regular jarred honey has been extracted from the comb by spinning or pressing, then often filtered and heated. With comb honey, you get the complete, unprocessed product exactly as it existed in the hive: honey, natural wax, bee pollen, propolis, and trace royal jelly. Nothing is added, removed, or altered.

---

### 2. Where exactly does this honey come from?

This honey is harvested from wild beehives (locally called *chaks*) in the Sundarbans — the world's largest mangrove forest, located in the coastal delta region of Bangladesh. The hives are built by wild bees in the trees and canopy of the forest, not in managed apiaries.

The honey is collected by traditional honey hunters known as **Moualis**, who have practiced this craft for generations. Collection is seasonal, primarily during the main honey season (roughly March to May), when forest flora is in full bloom.

---

### 3. Is this honey truly raw? What does "raw" mean here?

Yes. "Raw" means the honey has not been heated above the natural hive temperature (approximately 35°C), not pasteurized, and not filtered to remove pollen or other natural particles. It goes from the hive into the jar with no industrial processing.

This matters because heating and filtration destroy natural enzymes (like diastase and invertase), remove pollen, and degrade antioxidants and antimicrobial compounds. Raw honey retains all of these.

---

### 4. My honey has crystallized / turned solid. Is it spoiled?

No — crystallization is a sign of purity, not spoilage. Raw honey naturally crystallizes over time because of its high glucose content. The rate depends on floral source, temperature, and moisture content. Sundarbans honey may crystallize within weeks to a few months of purchase, especially in cooler conditions.

To re-liquefy: place the jar in warm water (not boiling — keep below 40°C) and stir gently. Do not microwave. Crystallized honey is completely safe to eat and retains all its nutritional properties.

### 5. How should I store this honey?

- Store at room temperature in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight.
- Keep the jar tightly sealed after opening to prevent moisture absorption, which can cause fermentation.
- Do not refrigerate — cold temperatures accelerate crystallization and are unnecessary since honey is naturally self-preserving.
- Do not store near heat sources or in a hot kitchen cabinet.

Properly stored, this honey will keep for **1–2 years or longer**. Honey does not truly "expire" if stored correctly, but flavor and enzyme activity are best within the first year.

### 6. Can I eat the wax comb? What do I do with it?

Yes, the comb is completely edible. The beeswax used to build natural honeycomb is food-grade and safe to consume. Most people chew the comb like gum — it releases residual honey as you chew, then leaves a soft waxy ball that you can either swallow (it passes through the digestive system harmlessly) or discard.

Some people prefer to scoop comb onto bread or cheese and eat it whole. Others chew it as a natural alternative to gum. There is no single correct way — it is a matter of preference.

Key things to check upon receiving your order:

- The honey should be **dark amber to golden-brown**, not pale yellow.
- It should appear **naturally cloudy** with visible particles — not crystal-clear.
- The comb should have **natural hexagonal cells** with some wax caps visible.
- The aroma should be **complex and earthy** with floral and forest notes.
- It will **crystallize** over time at room temperature.
- The taste should have a **rich, slow-building sweetness** with a mild bitter or astringent finish — not a sharp immediate sugar hit.

### 8. Why is this honey more expensive than honey at the supermarket?

Several reasons:

1. **Wild harvest:** Moualis risk their safety entering the Sundarbans, navigating tiger and crocodile territory, to hand-cut hives from trees. This cannot be mechanized.
2. **Seasonal and limited supply:** There is one primary harvest per year. Total yield is finite and weather-dependent.
3. **No processing costs, but high sourcing costs:** The honey is raw and unprocessed, but the collection, transport from a remote forest delta, and careful handling add to the cost.
4. **Complete product:** You are paying for honey, comb, pollen, propolis, and royal jelly together — not just extracted honey.

At BDT 1,500/kg, this product is actually significantly cheaper than comparable premium wild honeys internationally (Sidr, Manuka).

### 9. Is this honey available year-round?

No. This is a **seasonal product**. The primary harvest occurs roughly between **March and May**, when Sundarbans flora is in bloom and wild bee colonies are at peak honey production. Stock is limited to what is harvested each season.

Once the season's stock is sold out, the product may be unavailable until the following year's harvest. We recommend purchasing in sufficient quantity if you use it regularly.

### 10. Can I give this honey to my child?

**It depends on the child's age:**

- **Under 12 months: Absolutely not.** Raw honey — including this product — must never be given to infants under 1 year. It may contain *Clostridium botulinum* spores that can cause infant botulism, a potentially fatal illness.
- **Ages 1–3:** Generally safe after the first birthday, but introduce gradually in small amounts. Consult your pediatrician if unsure.
- **Ages 3 and above:** Safe for healthy children with no known bee product allergies.

See the full **warnings.md** document for complete safety information.

### 11. I have diabetes. Can I eat this honey?

Honey — including raw comb honey — is high in natural sugars (primarily fructose and glucose) and will raise blood glucose levels. It is **not a safe sugar substitute** for people managing diabetes without medical guidance.

Some studies suggest raw honey has a lower glycemic impact than refined sugar, but this does not make it safe for unrestricted consumption by diabetics. If you have diabetes, speak with your doctor or dietitian before incorporating this honey into your diet.

### 12. What health benefits does this honey have?

This honey contains naturally occurring compounds with documented properties:

- **Enzymes** (diastase, invertase, glucose oxidase): support digestion and antimicrobial activity.
- **Bee pollen**: source of protein, vitamins, and antioxidants.
- **Propolis**: natural antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory resin.
- **Hydrogen peroxide**: produced naturally by glucose oxidase; contributes to antibacterial activity.
- **Antioxidants**: flavonoids and phenolic compounds from Sundarbans flora.

Traditional uses include supporting immunity, soothing sore throats, aiding digestion, and wound healing.

**Important:** These are properties of the product based on its composition. This product is sold as a food, not a medicine. It should not be used to replace treatment for any medical condition. Do not make medical decisions based solely on traditional claims.

### 13. The honey has a slight fermented or tangy smell. Is this normal or is it bad?

A very faint fermented or earthy note is within the normal range for raw wild honey, particularly if moisture content is on the higher end or the honey was collected late in the season. The Sundarbans environment — humid, rich, diverse — can produce honey with complex aromatic notes that may seem unusual compared to mild commercial honey.

However, **discard the honey if you notice:**
- Active bubbling or fizzing when you open the jar
- A strong sour or alcoholic smell
- Visible mold on the comb or honey surface
- A noticeably alcohol-like taste

These are signs of actual fermentation, which can occur if the honey was harvested prematurely (high moisture content) or stored improperly with moisture exposure.

### 14. How much honey should I eat per day?

For healthy adults, **1–2 tablespoons (approximately 20–40g) per day** is a sensible moderate intake as part of a balanced diet. This is not a medical prescription — it is a general nutritional guideline.

At this level, honey contributes roughly 60–120 kcal and 15–30g of natural sugar. Honey counts toward your daily free sugar intake, which the WHO recommends keeping below 50g per day for an average adult.

There is no benefit to consuming large amounts. More is not better, and excessive honey consumption contributes to caloric surplus and dental issues just like any other sugar source.

### 15. Can I use this honey for cooking or baking?

You can, but with a caveat: **heating raw honey above approximately 40°C destroys its natural enzymes, antioxidants, and antimicrobial compounds** — the very properties that make it worth the premium price.

For cooking and baking at normal temperatures, a less expensive commercial honey will perform identically and save you money. We recommend using this honey **unheated** — on bread, in warm (not boiling) tea, on yogurt, with cheese, or eaten directly — to preserve the properties you are paying for.

### 16. I ordered the 500g size but the honey-to-comb ratio seems low. Is that correct?

The weight stated (250g, 500g, 1kg) refers to the **total product weight**, which includes both the honey and the natural beeswax comb. Since beeswax has its own weight, the volume of liquid honey in the jar will be somewhat less than the labeled weight might suggest.

This is normal and consistent across all comb honey products — the wax is part of the product, not filler. If you feel the ratio seems unusually skewed (mostly wax, very little honey visible in cells), that could indicate an issue worth raising with us directly.
