# Sourcing — Sundarbans Kholisha Flower Honey

*We think families deserve to know where their food comes from. Here is exactly how this honey gets from a wild forest hive in the Sundarbans to your door in Narayanganj.*

<section id="geographic-origin">

## Geographic Origin

This honey comes from the Sundarbans — the world's largest tidal mangrove forest, stretching across the southern delta of Bangladesh. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most ecologically protected areas on the planet. The Bangladeshi portion covers roughly 6,017 square kilometres across the districts of Khulna, Satkhira, and Bagerhat.

The specific floral source is the Kholisha plant (*Aegiceras corniculatum*), a salt-tolerant mangrove shrub that grows densely along tidal creek banks throughout the forest. It is not cultivated — it grows wild, as it has for centuries, in one of the most biodiverse coastal ecosystems in the world.

Wild bee colonies, primarily *Apis dorsata* (the giant honeybee), build their hives high in the mangrove canopy and forage across this ecosystem. The honey they produce reflects exactly where they live — the specific plants, the tidal air, the season.

This is not a product that can be replicated on a farm or produced anywhere else. It is genuinely local to this one place.

## Harvest Method

Honey collection in the Sundarbans is carried out by a traditional community called **Mouwalis** — hereditary honey collectors whose families have worked this forest for generations. Their knowledge of the forest, the hives, and the bees is not written down anywhere; it is passed from parent to child through direct experience.

**How collection works, step by step:**

**1. Permits first.** Before entering the forest, Mouwalis obtain seasonal collection permits from the Bangladesh Forest Department. Access is regulated — not everyone can enter, and not at all times of year.

**2. Into the forest by boat.** Collectors travel deep into the Sundarbans by small wooden boat, navigating the network of tidal rivers and creeks. Expeditions last several days to weeks depending on the permitted zone and how deep the active hives are.

**3. Locating hives.** Wild *Apis dorsata* hives are built on high branches of tall mangrove trees. Mouwalis find them using generational knowledge of the forest — reading light, tree type, bee flight patterns.

**4. Smoke to calm the bees.** Collectors burn specific natural materials to produce smoke that temporarily subdues bee activity. Traditional Mouwalis work with minimal protective clothing, relying on technique, timing, and experience rather than equipment.

**5. Partial comb collection.** A portion of the honeycomb is cut and collected. Responsible collectors — and the ones we work with — leave part of the comb intact so the colony survives and can rebuild. Full hive destruction is discouraged by both tradition and forest regulations.

**6. Straining on site.** Collected comb is strained through cloth to separate honey from wax and debris. This is done at the collection point or upon return to base. No heat is used.

**7. Into containers for transport.** Strained honey is stored in food-grade containers and transported out of the forest by boat to collection depots in Mongla or the broader Khulna region.

This work is genuinely difficult. Mouwalis face bee stings, saltwater crocodiles, and Bengal tigers on every expedition. The honey that reaches your family carries real human effort and real risk behind it.

## Season & Timing

Kholisha blooms for a short window each year — roughly **February to April**, with peak flowering in March. This is when bees forage most heavily on Kholisha nectar, producing honey with that variety's characteristic mild, floral flavour.

The Bangladesh Forest Department issues Mouwali collection permits aligned with this seasonal window. The exact dates vary year to year based on regulatory decisions and forest conditions.

When you order outside the February–April window, you are receiving honey from the most recent harvest season — not a different product. The harvest date is fixed; the availability is not.

## Supply Chain

Here is the honest path this honey takes from the forest to your home.

Wild hive — deep Sundarbans forest
↓
Mouwali collector — licensed, seasonal, generational knowledge
↓
Local collection depot — Mongla or Khulna district
↓
Supplier / aggregator — straining, storage, quality check
↓
Hasanah Mart — sourcing review, packaging, listing
↓
Your door — Narayanganj and whole Bangladesh

**What we want to be transparent about:**

The honey market in Bangladesh has real traceability problems. Once honey leaves the forest and enters aggregator networks, mixed-batch handling is common. A single wholesaler may combine honey from multiple collectors or seasons without disclosure. This is why we pay attention to which suppliers we work with — not just whether they can supply volume, but whether they can tell us something specific about where it came from.

We source from suppliers who maintain collector-level records where possible. We are not claiming a perfect, fully audited chain — that infrastructure does not yet exist at scale for Sundarbans honey in Bangladesh. What we are saying is that we ask the right questions, we do not buy from sources that cannot answer them, and if something about a batch concerns us, we do not list it.

**Shorter chain, better product.** The fewer hands honey passes through between the forest and your family, the lower the risk of dilution, mislabelling, or quality degradation. We keep our sourcing chain as short as we practically can — and we deliver directly to your home, so nothing sits in a shop for months before you buy it.
