## Who Should Avoid This Product

### Infants under 12 months — do not give honey under any circumstances

This is a firm medical guideline, not a precaution. Raw honey — and all honey — can contain spores of *Clostridium botulinum*, a bacterium that produces a toxin causing infant botulism. Adult digestive systems handle these spores safely. An infant's gut is not yet developed enough to do so.

Infant botulism is a serious illness. Symptoms include constipation, weakness, poor feeding, and in severe cases, difficulty breathing. It can be life-threatening.

**There is no safe amount of honey for babies under 12 months. Do not add it to food, milk, water, or a dummy/pacifier.** This applies regardless of honey type, origin, or processing method.

Children aged 1 year and above can consume honey safely in normal food quantities.

### Pregnant women

The evidence on honey consumption during pregnancy is not conclusive either way. Raw honey carries a very low but non-zero risk of bacterial contamination. Most doctors consider moderate consumption of honey safe during pregnancy, but individual circumstances vary. Consult your doctor or midwife if you are unsure.

### People with known bee or pollen allergies

As noted above, raw honey contains pollen and trace bee proteins. If you have a diagnosed bee venom allergy or significant seasonal pollen allergy, speak to your doctor before consuming raw honey.

### People who are immunocompromised

Individuals undergoing chemotherapy, organ transplant recipients, or anyone with a significantly weakened immune system should consult their doctor before consuming raw, unpasteurised honey. Pasteurised honey may be a safer option in these cases.

## Safe Usage Limits

Honey is a food, not a supplement or medicine. There is no officially established therapeutic dose. The following guidance is based on general dietary principles.

**For healthy adults:** 1 to 2 teaspoons (approximately 7–14 grams) per day is a reasonable everyday amount that adds sweetness without a significant sugar load. Consuming larger amounts regularly adds substantial natural sugar to your diet.

**For children over 1 year:** Small amounts — a teaspoon or less — as part of a normal varied diet. Honey should not be a primary source of sweetness in a child's daily diet.

**For children under 12 months:** None. See above.

**General principle:** Honey is high in natural sugar. Even pure, raw, high-quality honey consumed in large daily quantities contributes meaningfully to total sugar intake. For most families using honey as an occasional sweetener or flavouring, this is not a concern. For families watching sugar intake closely — particularly with diabetic or prediabetic members — keep portions small and consistent.
