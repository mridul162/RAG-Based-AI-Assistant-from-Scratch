**Simple home test — the spoon drop:** Place a small drop of honey on a dry ceramic plate. Authentic honey holds its shape as a neat mound. Adulterated or diluted honey spreads outward and absorbs into the surface quickly.

## Taste & Aroma Indicators

Kholisha honey has a distinctive but gentle character. Once you know it, it is recognisable.

**Aroma:** Delicate and faintly floral-woody, carrying the subtle scent of the mangrove bloom. It should not smell sharp, heavily sweet, or artificial. A caramel or burnt sugar smell is a sign the honey has been heated.

**Taste:** Mildly sweet with a clean floral finish. It is not aggressively sweet like sugar syrup, and it should not leave a sharp or artificial aftertaste. A very faint warmth in the throat after swallowing is normal for raw honey.

**Texture on the tongue:** Smooth and slightly coating. Watery or thin mouthfeel suggests dilution.

**What should concern you:**
- A taste that is intensely sweet with no floral complexity — likely sugar syrup or glucose blend
- A sour or fermented taste — indicates high moisture content or improper storage
- An oddly neutral flavour with no character — may be heavily processed or mixed-source honey repackaged as Kholisha

## Lab & Certification Checks

For families who want to go beyond visual and sensory checks, here is what laboratory verification actually looks like.

**What to know about certification first:** As of 2026, no active GI (Geographical Indication) certification exists for Sundarbans honey in Bangladesh. This means any seller can legally label their product "Sundarbans honey" regardless of actual origin. BSTI certification on packaging confirms a basic food safety standard but does not verify where the honey came from or which flowers the bees visited.

**Useful lab tests if you want independent verification:**

- **Pollen analysis (melissopalynology):** A specialised lab examines pollen present in the honey under a microscope. Genuine Kholisha honey will show *Aegiceras corniculatum* pollen. This is the most reliable method to confirm floral origin.
- **Moisture content test:** Raw honey should measure between 18–22% moisture. Higher than 20% increases fermentation risk; significantly higher suggests dilution or premature harvesting.
- **Sugar profile test (HPLC):** Detects added sucrose, high-fructose corn syrup, rice syrup, or other adulterants that alter the natural fructose-to-glucose ratio of pure honey.
- **HMF (Hydroxymethylfurfural) level:** Elevated HMF indicates the honey has been exposed to excessive heat. Low HMF confirms it has not been significantly heated.

## Common Fakes to Watch For

The Bangladeshi honey market has several well-documented adulteration patterns. Knowing them protects your family.

**Sugar syrup blending** is the most widespread issue. Cheap sugar syrup — sometimes glucose solution, sometimes rice syrup — is mixed into real honey to increase volume. At low ratios it is nearly impossible to detect by taste alone. The spoon test and crystallisation behaviour are your best home indicators.

**Heated honey sold as raw** is common in the mid-market. Sellers heat honey to make it look clearer, flow better, and resist crystallisation — which makes it easier to package and sell. The product looks premium but has lost much of what makes raw honey distinct. It will never crystallise naturally.

**Generic honey relabelled as Kholisha** is a straightforward repackaging fraud. Farmed multi-floral honey, or honey of unknown origin, is put into jars with Sundarbans branding. Without pollen testing there is no way to detect this visually. Your protection here is buying from sellers who can tell you something specific about their source — not just a label.

**Repackaged imported bulk honey** is less common but exists in the market. Large quantities of honey imported from abroad are locally rebottled and sold as domestic product. Look for Bangla harvest dates, seller contact details, and any sourcing reference on the packaging.
