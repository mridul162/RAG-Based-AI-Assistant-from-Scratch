## Allergen Information

<section id="allergens">

Black seed flower honey contains or may contain the following allergens:

<table>

| Allergen | Source | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|
| **Honey (bee products)** | Produced by *Apis* bees | Primary allergen; affects individuals with bee product sensitivity |
| **Bee pollen** | Naturally present in raw, unfiltered honey | Can trigger reactions in pollen-allergic individuals |
| **Nigella sativa pollen** | Specific to black seed flower nectar | May affect those sensitive to *Nigella sativa* or related Ranunculaceae family plants |
| **Propolis traces** | Naturally occurring in raw honey | Known allergen for some individuals |
| **Bee venom proteins (trace)** | Trace contamination possible in raw honey | Relevant for individuals with bee venom allergy |

</table>

### Notes on Allergen Risk

- This is a **raw, minimally processed honey**. It is not allergen-free and has not been processed to remove pollen.
- Individuals with known **hay fever, seasonal allergies, or pollen allergies** should start with a very small amount (¼ teaspoon) and monitor for reactions before regular use.
- Symptoms of a honey or pollen allergic reaction may include: itching or tingling of the mouth/throat, hives, swelling, sneezing, runny nose, or in severe cases, difficulty breathing.
- **If you experience any allergic symptoms, stop use immediately and seek medical attention.**
- This product is produced and packed in an environment that also handles other bee products. Cross-contact with other hive materials (beeswax, royal jelly, propolis) is possible.

## Who Should Avoid

<section id="contraindications">

The following groups should avoid this product entirely or consult a qualified healthcare provider before use:

### Absolute Contraindications

<table>

| Group | Reason |
|---|---|
| **Infants under 12 months** | Raw honey — including all floral varieties — can contain *Clostridium botulinum* spores. Infant digestive systems cannot neutralise these spores, creating a risk of infant botulism, a potentially fatal condition. **This warning has no exceptions.** |
| **Individuals with known honey allergy** | Direct contraindication; risk of anaphylaxis |
| **Individuals with known bee product allergy** | Includes allergy to bee pollen, propolis, or royal jelly |

</table>

### Consult a Doctor Before Use

<table>

| Group | Reason |
|---|---|
| **Pregnant women** | Raw honey carries a small risk of *Clostridium* spores and other microbial contamination; medical guidance recommended, particularly in the first trimester |
| **Breastfeeding women** | Limited safety data on nigella-derived compounds transmitted through breast milk |
| **Individuals with diabetes (Type 1 or Type 2)** | Honey is high in natural sugars (primarily fructose and glucose) and will affect blood glucose levels; use requires medical supervision and dose management |
| **Individuals with compromised immune systems** | Including those on immunosuppressant therapy, chemotherapy, or with HIV/AIDS; raw honey may carry microbial risks that a healthy immune system would neutralise |
| **Individuals with low blood pressure (hypotension)** | Black seed (*Nigella sativa*) compounds have demonstrated blood-pressure-lowering effects in some studies; compounding effect is possible |
| **Individuals scheduled for surgery** | Black seed has potential anticoagulant and blood-pressure effects; discontinue at least 2 weeks before any planned surgery |
| **Individuals with hormone-sensitive conditions** | Some research suggests nigella compounds may have mild hormonal activity; those with oestrogen-sensitive conditions should seek guidance |
| **Children under 3 years** | Though the infant botulism risk decreases significantly after 12 months, caution is advised for toddlers; consult a paediatrician |

</table>

## Drug / Condition Interactions

<section id="interactions">

Black seed flower honey carries the combined interaction profile of **raw honey** and **Nigella sativa** bioactive compounds (thymoquinone and related constituents present via nectar). The following interactions are documented in medical literature or are pharmacologically plausible.

<table>

| Drug / Medication Class | Nature of Interaction | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| **Anticoagulants / Blood thinners** (e.g. warfarin, heparin, aspirin) | Nigella sativa may have additive anticoagulant effects, increasing bleeding risk | Avoid or use only under medical supervision with regular INR monitoring |
| **Antidiabetic medications** (e.g. metformin, insulin, glibenclamide) | Honey raises blood glucose; nigella compounds may also lower blood glucose independently — combined effects on glycaemia are unpredictable | Requires dose adjustment guidance from a physician; do not self-manage |
| **Antihypertensive medications** (e.g. amlodipine, enalapril, losartan) | Nigella sativa has documented blood-pressure-lowering effects; combination may cause excessive hypotension | Monitor blood pressure closely; consult prescribing doctor |
| **Cytochrome P450-metabolised drugs** | Thymoquinone (from nigella) may affect CYP enzyme pathways, potentially altering how certain drugs are metabolised | Relevant for drugs with narrow therapeutic windows; consult a pharmacist |
| **Immunosuppressants** (e.g. cyclosporine, tacrolimus) | Nigella compounds have immunomodulatory properties that may interfere with intended immunosuppression | Avoid unless specifically approved by treating physician |
| **Chemotherapy agents** | Thymoquinone may interact with certain anticancer drugs; effects are not fully characterised | Discuss with oncologist before use |
| **Sedatives / CNS depressants** | Some evidence of mild sedative properties in nigella extracts; additive sedation possible | Use with caution; do not drive or operate machinery if combination causes drowsiness |

</table>

### Condition-Specific Notes

- **Kidney disease:** High-dose nigella consumption has been associated with nephrotoxic effects in animal studies. Individuals with pre-existing kidney conditions should use this honey cautiously and in small amounts only.
- **Liver disease:** Metabolic processing of nigella compounds may be impaired; consult a hepatologist.
- **Clotting disorders:** Any condition affecting blood coagulation (e.g. haemophilia, thrombocytopenia) warrants medical clearance before use.

> **Note:** The concentration of nigella bioactive compounds in flower honey is substantially lower than in black seed oil or supplements. Interaction risks are proportionally lower for typical dietary consumption (1–2 teaspoons/day), but the risk is not zero and is higher with medicinal or high-dose use.

</section>

## Safe Usage Limits

<section id="safe-limits">

### General Population (Healthy Adults)

<table>

| Context | Suggested Limit | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Daily dietary use** | 1–2 teaspoons (approximately 10–15g) per day | Consistent with general honey consumption guidance; accounts for total daily sugar intake |
| **Maximum single serving** | 1 tablespoon (approximately 20g) | Exceeding this regularly contributes to excess calorie and sugar intake |
| **Weekly upper limit** | No established specific limit; stay within daily guidance | Regular excess consumption contributes to weight gain, dental caries, and metabolic stress |

</table>

### Caloric and Sugar Reference

- 1 teaspoon (≈7g) of honey contains approximately **21 kcal** and **5.7g of sugars**
- 1 tablespoon (≈20g) contains approximately **60 kcal** and **16g of sugars**
- These values count toward the WHO recommendation of less than 25g of free sugars per day for adults

### Storage and Usage Safety

- **Do not heat in a microwave** or boil — high heat degrades beneficial compounds and can create HMF (hydroxymethylfurfural), which is harmful in large quantities
- **Do not add to boiling water or milk** — allow liquids to cool to below 40°C before adding honey
- **Store in a sealed container away from direct sunlight and moisture** — improper storage encourages fermentation
- **Do not use from a wet spoon** — introducing water into the jar promotes yeast growth and spoilage
- **Crystallised honey is safe to consume** — gently warm the sealed jar in warm water (not boiling) to re-liquefy

### Shelf Life Note

Honey does not have a strict expiry date if stored correctly in a sealed container. However, this product should be consumed within **24 months of harvest** for optimal quality. If the honey smells sour, fermented, or fizzy, discard it.
