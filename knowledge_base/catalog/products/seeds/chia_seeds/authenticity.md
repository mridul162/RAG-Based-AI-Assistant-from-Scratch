> This document helps customers verify whether their Chia Seeds (চিয়া বীজ) are genuine *Salvia hispanica* seeds and not a substituted, adulterated, or misrepresented product. Use these checks on delivery and before regular consumption.

---

## Visual Indicators — দৃশ্যমান সংকেত

<section id="visual">

Genuine chia seeds have a consistent and identifiable appearance. Because they are small and unfamiliar to many buyers in Bangladesh, visual verification is an important first step.

| Attribute | Authentic Chia Seeds | Suspect / Likely Substituted |
|---|---|---|
| **Size** | Small oval seeds, approximately 1–2mm in length | Significantly smaller (may be sabja/tukma) or larger than expected |
| **Color** | Speckled pattern — mix of black, dark grey, and white markings on each seed | Uniformly solid black throughout (likely sabja seeds); uniformly brown (may be flaxseeds) |
| **Shape** | Consistently oval, slightly flattened | Round and uniform (sabja); elongated and flat (flaxseeds) |
| **Surface** | Smooth with visible mottled pattern under good light | No visible pattern — solid color only |
| **Dry texture** | Free-flowing, no clumping in dry state | Heavy clumping in dry state suggests moisture exposure or different seed type |
| **Dust / debris** | Minimal fine dust; mostly whole seeds | Excessive powder, broken seeds, or visible foreign matter |
| **Uniformity** | Reasonably consistent size and color across the batch | Large variation in seed size or presence of seeds from multiple plant types |

> **White chia seeds:** A fully white or cream-colored variety of chia (*Salvia hispanica*) does exist and is genuine. However, pure white chia is less common in the Bangladeshi import market. If your seeds are entirely white with no dark seeds at all, verify with the seller that white chia was specifically sourced — this is not necessarily a sign of adulteration, but worth confirming.

</section>

## Taste & Aroma Indicators — স্বাদ ও গন্ধের সংকেত

<section id="sensory">

One of chia seeds' defining characteristics is their near-complete neutrality in flavor. This is both a feature and a verification point.

**Aroma (dry seeds):**
- Very faint, mild, slightly nutty or grain-like smell
- No strong odor — not sharp, not pungent, not rancid
- Rancid or sour smell indicates oxidized oils from poor storage or old stock — do not consume
- A strong herb-like or basil-adjacent aroma may indicate sabja seeds

**Taste (dry or soaked):**
- Virtually flavorless — this is correct and expected
- Very faint, neutral, slightly starchy taste at most
- No bitterness, no sharpness, no distinctive flavor
- If seeds have a noticeably nutty or bitter taste, they may be flaxseeds
- If seeds have an earthy or herbal note, they may be sabja/tukma

**Texture when soaked (the most reliable sensory test):**
- Place 1 tablespoon in half a cup of water and wait 20–30 minutes
- Genuine chia seeds absorb water and form a thick, even, transparent gel coating around each seed — the mucilage layer
- The seed itself remains intact and firm inside the gel
- The resulting mixture should be viscous, pudding-like, and hold its shape loosely
- Sabja seeds gel faster (5–10 minutes) and produce a slightly different, thinner gel
- Flaxseeds do not gel in whole form

**Quick sensory verification summary:**
1. Smell dry seeds — should be faint and neutral, not sharp or rancid
2. Taste a few dry seeds — should be almost flavorless
3. Soak in water for 30 minutes — should form a thick, clear gel around each seed
4. The gel should be stable and not dissolve or become watery after an hour

</section>

## Lab / Certification Checks — পরীক্ষা ও সনদ

<section id="certification">

Chia seeds sold in Bangladesh are almost entirely imported, which makes formal domestic certification limited. Here is what to look for and ask about:

**Import and quality documentation to request:**

| Check | What It Confirms |
|---|---|
| **Country of origin certificate** | Verifies the declared source country (Australia, India, Mexico, Bolivia, etc.) |
| **Phytosanitary certificate** | Confirms seeds were inspected for pests and disease at origin — required for legal import into Bangladesh |
| **Moisture content** | Should be below 10–12% for safe storage; high moisture causes clumping and mold risk |
| **Foreign matter / purity grade** | Commercial grade chia should be 98–99%+ pure seed with minimal debris or foreign material |
| **Heavy metal / pesticide residue test** | Relevant for imported food; ask if the importer has a lab report for the batch |
| **BSTI clearance** | Imported food products should have cleared Bangladesh Standards and Testing Institution import requirements |

**International certifications to look for (if present on packaging):**
- **Organic certification** (USDA Organic, EU Organic, or equivalent) — confirms seeds were grown without synthetic pesticides. This is optional and not all quality chia is organic, but it is a meaningful marker if present.
- **ISO 22000 or FSSC 22000** — food safety management certification at the processing/packing facility level
- **Non-GMO verified** — chia seeds are not a GMO crop by any current commercial standard, so this claim, while common, is largely redundant; its absence is not a concern

**Practical note for most buyers:** The majority of chia seeds in the Bangladeshi retail market do not come with visible certifications on packaging. The most practical verification steps for regular consumers are the sensory and visual checks above, combined with purchasing from a seller who can identify the country of origin and approximate import date of their current stock.

</section>

## Common Fakes to Watch For — ভেজাল ও প্রতারণা চেনার উপায়

<section id="adulteration">

The chia seed market in Bangladesh has a specific and well-known substitution problem, along with a few other quality issues worth knowing.

---

### 1. Sabja / Tukma Seeds Sold as Chia Seeds (সাবজা বীজকে চিয়া বলে বিক্রি)

**What it is:** The most common substitution in the Bangladeshi market. Sabja seeds (*Ocimum basilicum* / sweet basil seeds) are cheaper, more locally available, and look superficially similar to chia seeds to an unfamiliar buyer. Some sellers knowingly mislabel them; others may themselves be misinformed.

**How to spot it:**
- Seeds are uniformly solid black with no speckled pattern
- Seeds are slightly smaller and more uniform in size than chia
- They gel significantly faster — within 5–10 minutes versus 20–30 for chia
- They have a faint earthy or basil-like aroma when smelled closely
- Under good lighting or a magnifying glass, genuine chia seeds show a visible mottled or marbled surface pattern — sabja seeds do not

**Why it matters:** Sabja seeds are not dangerous, but they have a different nutritional profile — lower in Omega-3, fiber, and calcium than chia. If you are buying chia for specific nutritional reasons, substitution defeats the purpose.

---

### 2. Flaxseeds Mixed Into or Substituted for Chia Seeds (তিসি বীজ মেশানো)

**What it is:** Flaxseeds (*Linum usitatissimum*) are flat, oval, and brown or golden in color — quite different from chia visually, but occasionally mixed into chia in lower-quality or loose/bulk purchases.

**How to spot it:**
- Flaxseeds are noticeably larger and flatter than chia seeds
- They are solid brown or golden — no speckle pattern
- They have a distinct nutty, slightly oily smell and taste
- They do not form a mucilage gel in whole form when soaked

---

### 3. Old or Oxidized Stock (পুরনো বা নষ্ট মজুত)

**What it is:** Chia seeds contain high levels of Omega-3 fatty acids, which can oxidize and go rancid over time — particularly if stored in warm, humid, or light-exposed conditions. Rancid chia seeds are not a substitution but are a quality failure.

**How to spot it:**
- Smell is noticeably sour, sharp, paint-like, or unpleasant
- Seeds may be clumped in the dry bag from humidity exposure
- Gel formed when soaking may smell off or fermented
- Color may appear duller or more faded than fresh stock

**What to do:** Do not consume rancid seeds. Check the best-before date on packaging and ask the seller for the import/production date if the packaging does not clearly show it.

---

### 4. Underweight Packaging (কম ওজন দেওয়া)

**What it is:** A general market issue with loose or repackaged goods — the stated weight on the label does not match the actual contents.

**How to spot it:**
- Weigh the contents (without packaging) on a kitchen scale upon delivery
- A 500g pack should contain approximately 500g of seeds, allowing for minor variance of ±5g
- Significant shortfall (more than 5–10%) on a food product is a quality and consumer rights issue

---

### Quick Verification Checklist

| Step | What to Do | Genuine Result |
|---|---|---|
| **Visual check** | Examine seeds under good light | Speckled black-white-grey pattern visible |
| **Size check** | Compare to a sesame seed | Chia is slightly smaller than sesame, oval shaped |
| **Smell check** | Smell dry seeds | Neutral to very faint — no sharpness or herb smell |
| **Gel test** | 1 tbsp in ½ cup water, wait 30 min | Thick, clear gel coating; holds shape |
| **Taste test** | Chew a few dry seeds | Virtually flavorless |
| **Weight check** | Weigh contents without packaging | Within ±5g of stated weight |

> ⚠️ If you suspect substitution or adulteration in a commercial purchase, contact the seller first with photos and your observations. For formal disputes, BSTI and the Directorate of National Consumer Rights Protection (DNCRP) in Bangladesh handle consumer complaints about mislabeled food products.
