## Allergen Information

<section id="allergens">

| Allergen | Present | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Milk (Dairy)** | ✅ Yes — primary ingredient | Derived from deshi cow milk; contains trace milk proteins |
| **Casein (A1)** | ❌ Not present (by product definition) | Sourced from A2 beta-casein cows only; does not contain A1 casein |
| **Lactose** | ⚠️ Trace amounts possible | Pure ghee has most lactose removed during clarification, but trace residues may remain — not guaranteed lactose-free |
| **Whey proteins** | ⚠️ Trace amounts possible | Removed during the slow-cooking/skimming process, but not to pharmaceutical-grade zero |
| **Gluten** | ❌ Not an ingredient | No wheat or gluten-containing ingredients; cross-contamination risk depends on production facility |
| **Soy, nuts, eggs** | ❌ Not an ingredient | Not used in production |

**Key clarification on A2 and dairy allergy:**
- A2 ghee is **not safe for people with a confirmed cow's milk protein allergy (CMPA)**. The A2 designation refers to the *type* of beta-casein protein, not the absence of dairy allergens.
- People with **lactose intolerance** (not milk allergy) may tolerate pure ghee well due to the near-complete removal of lactose, but individual tolerance varies. This is not a medical guarantee.
- People with a **whey protein sensitivity** should exercise caution, as trace whey may remain.

## Who Should Avoid

<section id="avoid">

The following groups should avoid or significantly restrict consumption and consult a doctor first:

**Absolute caution / likely avoid:**

- **Individuals with confirmed cow's milk protein allergy (CMPA):** Even trace dairy proteins in ghee can trigger allergic reactions, including anaphylaxis in severe cases.
- **Individuals with hyperlipidemia (high LDL cholesterol) or familial hypercholesterolemia:** Ghee is predominantly saturated fat (~60–65%). Regular consumption may worsen lipid profiles in those with existing conditions.
- **Individuals with cardiovascular disease (CVD) or history of heart attack/stroke:** High saturated fat intake may be contraindicated depending on the clinical profile. Physician guidance required.
- **Individuals with gallbladder disease or gallstones:** High-fat foods stimulate bile release and may provoke symptoms or attacks.
- **Individuals with pancreatitis (acute or chronic):** High dietary fat is a known trigger. Avoid during active episodes; restrict even in remission unless medically cleared.
- **Individuals with obesity (BMI >30) on calorie-restricted diets:** Ghee is calorie-dense (~900 kcal per 100g). Even small amounts add significant calories.

**Use with caution:**

- **Infants under 6 months:** Should receive only breast milk or formula. Ghee is not appropriate as a food or supplement for this age group.
- **Infants 6–12 months:** Ghee may be introduced in very small quantities as part of complementary feeding (a common South Asian practice), but only under pediatric guidance. Do not use as a primary fat source.
- **Pregnant women:** Moderate consumption is generally considered safe in South Asian dietary traditions, but excessive intake of saturated fat during pregnancy is not recommended. Consult a gynecologist or registered dietitian.
- **Individuals with Type 2 diabetes or insulin resistance:** Ghee has a negligible glycemic impact, but its high saturated fat content may affect insulin sensitivity over time. Portion control is important.
- **Individuals with liver disease (fatty liver, cirrhosis):** High fat intake may place additional stress on a compromised liver.
- **Elderly individuals with sedentary lifestyles:** Caloric density and saturated fat content warrant careful portioning.

## Drug / Condition Interactions

<section id="interactions">

> The interactions below are based on the nutritional composition of ghee (predominantly saturated fat, fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, K). Always inform your doctor or pharmacist about dietary fat intake when on the following medications.

| Drug / Condition | Nature of Interaction | Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| **Statins** (e.g., atorvastatin, rosuvastatin) | Ghee's saturated fat may partially counteract LDL-lowering effects | Discuss dietary fat targets with your cardiologist |
| **Warfarin / Anticoagulants** | Vitamin K in ghee can affect INR stability in patients on blood thinners | Maintain consistent ghee intake; sudden large increases may affect clotting |
| **Fat-soluble vitamin supplements** (A, D, E, K) | Ghee enhances absorption of fat-soluble vitamins — beneficial in normal amounts, but may cause over-supplementation if combined with high-dose supplements | Monitor vitamin D and A levels if supplementing alongside regular ghee use |
| **Lipid-lowering drugs** (fibrates, bile acid sequestrants) | High saturated fat intake may reduce drug efficacy | Consult prescribing physician about dietary fat limits |
| **Orlistat** (weight-loss drug that blocks fat absorption) | Orlistat combined with high-fat foods including ghee increases risk of gastrointestinal side effects (oily stools, urgency, cramps) | Avoid or minimize fat intake during Orlistat treatment |
| **Cholestyramine / Colestipol** | These bile acid binders interact with dietary fat timing | Follow physician's dietary guidance on fat intake timing around dosing |
| **Hypothyroidism** | Excess saturated fat may affect thyroid hormone metabolism in some individuals | Monitor thyroid levels; discuss dietary fat with endocrinologist |

**No known interaction with:**
- Common antibiotics, antihistamines, or OTC pain relievers at normal dietary intake levels.

</section>

## Safe Usage Limits

<section id="safe-limits">

**General population (healthy adults):**

- The American Heart Association and similar guidelines recommend keeping saturated fat to **less than 10% of total daily calories** (approximately 20–22g for a 2,000 kcal diet).
- One teaspoon of ghee (~5g) contains approximately **4–4.5g of saturated fat**.
- **Practical guideline: 1–2 teaspoons per day (5–10g)** is widely considered a moderate and reasonable amount for healthy adults using ghee as a cooking fat or condiment in the South Asian dietary context.
- This is not a therapeutic dose recommendation — it is a general dietary reference.

**Calorie reference:**

| Serving | Approximate Weight | Approximate Calories |
|---|---|---|
| 1 teaspoon | ~5g | ~45 kcal |
| 1 tablespoon | ~15g | ~135 kcal |
| Standard cooking use (1 meal) | ~10–15g | ~90–135 kcal |

**Children:**
- Children over 2 years with no dairy allergy can consume ghee as part of a balanced diet in age-appropriate small amounts.
- Do not use ghee as a weight-gain supplement for children without pediatric supervision.

**Storage safety note:**
- Improperly stored ghee (exposed to moisture, stored in non-airtight containers, or kept in warm and humid conditions) can become rancid. **Rancid ghee should not be consumed.** Signs include an off, sour, or metallic smell and a darker-than-normal color. Discard if in doubt.
- Recommended storage: airtight glass jar, away from direct sunlight, at room temperature (if consumed within 2–3 months) or refrigerated for longer shelf life.

**Heating safety:**
- Ghee has a high smoke point (~230–250°C / 450–480°F), making it suitable for high-heat cooking. However, **burning ghee produces harmful compounds** — do not heat until smoking.

</section>
