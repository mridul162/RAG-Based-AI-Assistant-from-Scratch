## Visual Indicators

<section id="visual">

| Property | Authentic | Suspect / Adulterated |
|---|---|---|
| **Color** | Deep golden-yellow; may appear slightly orange-gold in warm conditions | Pale yellow, white, or unnaturally bright — may indicate vegetable fat blending |
| **Texture at room temp (≤25°C)** | Semi-solid to solid with a visible grainy or crystalline texture (granular solidification is a hallmark of pure desi cow ghee) | Completely smooth, creamy, or soft like butter — suggests hydrogenated fat or buffalo ghee |
| **Texture in heat** | Melts cleanly and quickly into a clear liquid with no cloudiness | Turns cloudy, foamy, or leaves white residue — indicates water content or starch |
| **Separation** | No visible water separation; no white sediment at the bottom after cooling | Water droplets or white chalky layer at the bottom |
| **Clarity when melted** | Crystal-clear liquid, deep amber-gold | Hazy, milky, or overly pale when fully liquefied |
| **Jar/container** | Ghee should not have an oily film on the inner glass above the ghee line | Oily residue streaking high up the jar may indicate liquid oil was mixed in |

**Quick home check:** Place a small amount on your palm. Authentic A2 desi cow ghee melts almost immediately from body heat. If it stays solid or takes a long time, it may contain hydrogenated vegetable fat (dalda).

## Taste & Aroma Indicators

<section id="sensory">

**Aroma**
- Authentic Pabnar ghee has a **rich, nutty, and distinctly cooked-milk aroma** — warm and deep, not sharp or chemically perfumed.
- The smell intensifies noticeably when a small amount is heated in a pan. It should smell like slow-cooked clarified butter, not like coconut oil, vegetable shortening, or artificial flavoring.
- Ghee from Pabnar indigenous (deshi) cows often carries a **slightly grassy, pastoral undertone** due to the char-area grazing diet of the cows — this is a positive authenticity marker.
- If the smell is bland, neutral, or smells like refined oil, treat it as a red flag.

**Taste**
- The flavor is **rich, full-bodied, and slightly nutty** with a clean, long-lasting finish on the palate.
- There should be **no greasy, waxy, or soapy aftertaste** — these are signs of vegetable fat or stearic acid adulteration.
- A faint natural sweetness from the milk solids is normal and desirable.
- Saltiness of any kind is abnormal — pure ghee contains no salt.

**Mouthfeel**
- Melts smoothly and completely with no grittiness (small natural granules from crystallization are normal when solid, but should dissolve cleanly in the mouth).
- Should not leave a thick, coating, or oily film on the tongue or the roof of the mouth.

</section>

## Lab / Certification Checks

<section id="certification">

No single home test replaces lab verification. The following methods are recognized:

**Recommended Lab Tests**

| Test | What It Detects | Method |
|---|---|---|
| **Reichert-Meissl (RM) Value** | Confirms pure cow ghee (RM value 26–32 for cow ghee; lower values indicate vegetable fat blending) | Titrimetric — standard BSTI/FSSAI method |
| **Baudouin Test** | Detects sesame oil adulteration | Add furfural + HCl — pink/red color = sesame oil present |
| **Halphen Test** | Detects cottonseed oil | Red color development on heating with amyl alcohol + sulfur |
| **Phytosterol Test** | Distinguishes animal fat from plant-based fat | GC or colorimetric |
| **Beta-casein A2 milk verification** | Confirms milk source is A2 cows (deshi indigenous breed) | PCR-based breed or milk protein analysis — specialized lab required |
| **Moisture content** | Pure ghee: ≤0.5% moisture (BSTI standard) | Karl Fischer titration or oven-drying method |
| **Free Fatty Acid (FFA)** | Checks for rancidity and old fat reprocessing | Titration; FFA >0.5% is a concern |

**Certifications to look for:**
- **BSTI (Bangladesh Standards and Testing Institution)** mark on packaging — mandatory for commercially sold ghee in Bangladesh.
- Batch number, production date, and expiry clearly printed (not stickered over).
- Supplier traceability documentation (farm/collection region, processing date).

> ⚠️ No current international A2 certification body operates widely in Bangladesh. Claims of "A2 certified" without BSTI or third-party lab backing should be verified directly with the seller.

</section>

## Common Fakes to Watch For

<section id="fakes">

Adulteration of deshi cow ghee is common in the Bangladeshi market. The following are the most frequently reported methods:

**1. Dalda (Hydrogenated Vegetable Fat) Blending**
The most widespread adulteration. Dalda is cheap, solid at room temperature, and visually similar to ghee.
- *How to spot:* The ghee will be smoother and less granular than pure product. When melted, it may appear slightly cloudy. The RM value in lab tests will be significantly below 26.

**2. Buffalo Ghee Substitution or Blending**
Buffalo ghee is cheaper than deshi cow ghee and has a similar appearance. It is often passed off as cow ghee.
- *How to spot:* Buffalo ghee is whiter in color and has a milder, less complex aroma. It solidifies very smoothly (no granular texture). The Baudouin-style phytosterol panel can distinguish the two.

**3. Coconut Oil or Palm Oil Addition**
Liquid oils are sometimes added to increase volume.
- *How to spot:* The melted ghee will appear too fluid and thin. A strong coconut or neutral vegetable-oil smell will be detectable. There may be visible oily separation when the product cools.

**4. Added Artificial Color or Flavoring**
Turmeric or synthetic food dye is sometimes added to mimic the golden color of pure cow ghee. Artificial "ghee flavor" is used to mask neutral base fats.
- *How to spot:* Place a small amount on white tissue paper. Artificial color will bleed into the paper. Smell before heating — a perfumed or chemical note instead of a cooked-milk aroma is a warning sign.

**5. Water or Chhana (Cottage Cheese) Whey Addition**
Adds weight; very low-cost fraud.
- *How to spot:* Bubbling or sputtering when a small amount is heated in a dry pan (water content). White residue at the bottom of the jar after the product cools. Moisture content in lab test will exceed 0.5%.

**6. Reprocessed or Rancid Fat**
Old, expired, or rancid ghee is re-melted, re-filtered, and sold as fresh.
- *How to spot:* Off-smell — musty, sour, or stale rather than nutty. High FFA value in lab testing. Shorter shelf life after opening.

**Practical buyer checklist before purchasing:**

- [ ] Is the product sold with a BSTI mark and a clear batch/date?
- [ ] Is the color deep golden (not pale or white)?
- [ ] Does it smell rich and nutty — not perfumed, oily, or neutral?
- [ ] Is the texture granular/crystalline when solid (not creamy smooth)?
- [ ] Can the seller provide sourcing information (Pabna region, deshi cow)?
- [ ] Does it melt quickly and clearly on a warm surface?
