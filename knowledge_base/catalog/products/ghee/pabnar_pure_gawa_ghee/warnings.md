## Allergen Information

<section id="allergens">

| Allergen | Present | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Milk (Dairy)** | ✅ Yes — primary ingredient | Derived entirely from deshi cow milk; dairy is inherent to this product |
| **Lactose** | ⚠️ Trace amounts possible | The slow-cooking/clarification process removes most lactose, but trace residues may remain — this product is **not guaranteed lactose-free** |
| **Casein & whey proteins** | ⚠️ Trace amounts possible | Substantially removed during the bilona/gawa clarification process, but not to pharmaceutical-grade zero; individuals with milk protein allergy should treat this as a dairy product |
| **Gluten** | ❌ Not an ingredient | No wheat or gluten-containing ingredients used; cross-contamination risk depends on the production and storage environment — not tested or certified gluten-free |
| **Tree nuts, soy, eggs, sesame** | ❌ Not ingredients | Not used in production of pure gawa ghee |

**Important clarifications:**

- **Dairy allergy vs. lactose intolerance are not the same thing.** People with lactose intolerance (difficulty digesting lactose) may tolerate pure ghee in moderate amounts due to its very low lactose content. People with a **cow's milk protein allergy (CMPA)** — an immune response to milk proteins such as casein or whey — should treat this product as unsafe regardless of the clarification process, as trace proteins remain.
- **Anaphylaxis risk:** Individuals with a confirmed severe dairy allergy should avoid this product entirely. Even trace protein residues can trigger a serious allergic reaction in sensitized individuals.
- If you are unsure whether you have lactose intolerance or a milk protein allergy, consult an allergist before consuming.

## Who Should Avoid

<section id="avoid">

**Avoid entirely — high risk:**

- **Individuals with confirmed cow's milk protein allergy (CMPA):** Trace dairy proteins in ghee can trigger allergic reactions ranging from digestive distress to anaphylaxis in severely sensitized individuals.
- **Individuals with severe hyperlipidemia or familial hypercholesterolemia:** Gawa ghee is predominantly saturated fat (~60–65%). Unrestricted use may significantly worsen LDL cholesterol levels and cardiovascular risk in this group.
- **Individuals with acute pancreatitis:** High dietary fat is a well-established trigger for pancreatitis flare-ups. Avoid during active episodes without explicit medical clearance.
- **Individuals with active gallbladder disease or gallstones (cholelithiasis):** Dietary fat stimulates gallbladder contraction and bile release, which can provoke pain or complications.

**Use with caution — consult a doctor first:**

- **Infants under 6 months:** Should receive only breast milk or approved formula. Ghee is not appropriate as food or supplement for this age group under any circumstances.
- **Infants 6–12 months:** Small amounts as part of traditional complementary feeding is a common South Asian practice, but only under pediatric guidance. Do not use as a primary fat source or caloric supplement without a doctor's recommendation.
- **Pregnant women:** Moderate consumption is generally compatible with a healthy pregnancy in South Asian dietary traditions. However, excessive saturated fat intake during pregnancy is not advisable. Consult a gynecologist or registered dietitian for personalized guidance.
- **Individuals with type 2 diabetes or insulin resistance:** Ghee has negligible direct glycemic impact, but its high saturated fat content may negatively affect insulin sensitivity over time with excessive use. Portion discipline is important.
- **Individuals with cardiovascular disease (CVD), history of heart attack, or stroke:** Saturated fat intake targets are often strictly managed in these patients. Physician guidance on dietary fat limits is required before regular ghee use.
- **Individuals with non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) or cirrhosis:** High fat intake may place additional metabolic burden on an already compromised liver.
- **Individuals with obesity (BMI >30) on calorie-restricted plans:** Ghee is calorie-dense (~900 kcal per 100g). Small volumes carry significant caloric weight and must be accounted for in dietary tracking.
- **Elderly individuals with sedentary lifestyles:** Caloric density and saturated fat content warrant careful portioning and ideally dietitian input.
- **Children under 2 years:** While ghee is traditional in South Asian infant feeding, amounts and timing should always follow pediatric guidance specific to the child's weight, health, and developmental stage.

</section>

## Drug / Condition Interactions

<section id="interactions">

> The interactions below stem from ghee's nutritional composition: predominantly saturated fat with naturally occurring fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K). Always disclose dietary fat intake to your doctor or pharmacist when on the following medications or managing the following conditions.

| Drug / Condition | Nature of Interaction | Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| **Statins** (atorvastatin, rosuvastatin, simvastatin, etc.) | Regular high intake of saturated fat may partially counteract the LDL-lowering effect of statin therapy | Discuss dietary fat targets with your cardiologist; moderate use is likely fine for most patients on statins |
| **Warfarin / other anticoagulants** (blood thinners) | Ghee contains Vitamin K, which plays a role in blood clotting. Sudden large increases in Vitamin K intake can destabilize INR in patients on warfarin | Maintain consistent ghee intake rather than suddenly increasing or eliminating it; inform your prescribing doctor |
| **Fat-soluble vitamin supplements** (A, D, E, K) | Dietary fat significantly enhances absorption of fat-soluble vitamins. Consuming high-dose supplements alongside regular ghee use may lead to over-absorption, particularly of Vitamins A and D, which are toxic in excess | Monitor serum Vitamin D and A levels if supplementing; avoid mega-dose fat-soluble vitamins without medical supervision |
| **Orlistat** (fat-absorption blocking weight-loss drug) | Orlistat works by preventing dietary fat absorption. Consuming ghee or other fats while on Orlistat causes predictable and uncomfortable gastrointestinal side effects: oily stools, fecal urgency, and abdominal cramps | Minimize or avoid fat intake during Orlistat treatment; discuss dietary fat limits with your prescribing physician |
| **Fibrates / bile acid sequestrants** (e.g., cholestyramine, colestipol) | These lipid-lowering drugs interact with dietary fat metabolism and timing | Follow the dietary guidance provided by your prescribing physician regarding fat intake around dosing times |
| **Corticosteroids** (long-term use) | Long-term corticosteroid use raises cardiovascular risk; adding high saturated fat intake compounds this risk | Discuss dietary fat targets with your physician if on long-term steroid therapy |
| **Hypothyroidism** | Some evidence suggests excess dietary saturated fat may affect thyroid hormone metabolism and lipid clearance in hypothyroid patients | Monitor thyroid function and cholesterol levels; discuss dietary fat with your endocrinologist |
| **Chronic kidney disease (CKD)** | High saturated fat intake may worsen dyslipidemia, which is already a significant concern in CKD patients | Dietary fat restrictions in CKD are highly individualized; follow your nephrologist's or renal dietitian's guidance |

**No clinically significant interaction expected with:** standard antibiotics, antihistamines, common OTC pain relievers (paracetamol, ibuprofen), or antacids at normal dietary intake levels of ghee.

</section>

## Safe Usage Limits

<section id="safe-limits">

**General guidance for healthy adults:**

- Major health bodies (including the American Heart Association and WHO) recommend keeping saturated fat to **less than 10% of total daily caloric intake** — approximately 20–22g for a standard 2,000 kcal diet.
- One teaspoon of ghee (~5g) contains approximately **4–4.5g of saturated fat**.
- A practical, widely referenced guideline for healthy adults: **1–2 teaspoons per day (5–10g)** used as a cooking fat or condiment. This fits within a balanced diet without exceeding saturated fat recommendations for most people.
- This is a general dietary reference, not a therapeutic dosage or a blanket permission for unrestricted use.

**Calorie reference table:**

| Serving | Approximate Weight | Approximate Calories | Approx. Saturated Fat |
|---|---|---|---|
| ½ teaspoon | ~2.5g | ~22 kcal | ~2g |
| 1 teaspoon | ~5g | ~45 kcal | ~4g |
| 1 tablespoon | ~15g | ~135 kcal | ~12g |
| Typical single-meal cooking use | ~10–15g | ~90–135 kcal | ~8–12g |

**For children (over 2 years, no dairy allergy):**
Ghee in small, age-appropriate amounts as part of a varied diet is generally considered safe and is a traditional part of South Asian cuisine. It should not be used as a primary caloric supplement or weight-gain intervention without pediatric oversight.

**Storage safety:**
- Store in an airtight glass or food-grade container away from heat, sunlight, and moisture.
- Use a clean, dry spoon at all times — moisture introduced into the container accelerates spoilage.
- At room temperature (below 28–30°C): best consumed within **2–3 months** of opening.
- Refrigerated: shelf life extends to **6–12 months**.
- **Do not consume rancid ghee.** Signs of rancidity: sour, musty, metallic, or stale smell; unpleasant aftertaste; abnormally dark color. Discard immediately if any of these are present.

**Cooking temperature safety:**
- Gawa ghee has a high smoke point (~230–250°C / 450–480°F), making it suitable for sautéing, tempering, and most stovetop cooking.
- **Do not heat until smoking or burning.** Overheated ghee produces harmful oxidation byproducts. If ghee begins to smoke heavily, remove from heat immediately and discard that batch.

</section>
