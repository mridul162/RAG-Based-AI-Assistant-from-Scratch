## Visual Indicators

<section id="visual">

| Property | Authentic | Suspect / Adulterated |
|---|---|---|
| **Color** | Rich golden to deep amber-yellow; may carry a slight orange tint in warm months | Pale yellow, off-white, or unnaturally uniform — suggests vegetable fat, buffalo ghee, or artificial coloring |
| **Texture at room temp (≤25°C)** | Semi-solid to fully solid with a distinctly grainy or crystalline surface — this is the defining physical signature of pure gawa ghee | Smooth, creamy, or butter-like when solid — strongly suggests hydrogenated fat (dalda) or buffalo ghee blending |
| **Texture when melted** | Melts into a clear, clean, deep amber liquid with no cloudiness or residue | Cloudy, milky, or leaving white film when fully melted — indicates water, starch, or non-dairy fat |
| **Granularity** | Visible natural crystals when cooled, especially near the sides and bottom of the jar | No crystals at any temperature — too smooth and uniform |
| **Jar interior** | No oily streaking on jar walls above the ghee surface level | Oily film coating the glass well above the ghee line — may indicate liquid oil was added to increase volume |
| **Settling** | No water separation or white chalky deposit at the bottom | White sediment, water pooling, or cloudy layer at the bottom of the jar |

**Quick home checks:**

- **Palm test:** Place a small quantity on your palm. Pure gawa ghee melts almost immediately from body heat alone. Slow or incomplete melting suggests hydrogenated fat content.
- **Paper test:** Rub a small amount on clean white paper. Pure ghee leaves a light, translucent, non-sticky oil mark. Artificial color will visibly bleed into the paper.
- **Heat test:** Heat a dry pan and add a small spoonful. Pure ghee should melt quickly, turn golden, and remain clear. Sputtering or bubbling indicates water content; cloudiness suggests non-dairy fat.

## Taste & Aroma Indicators

<section id="sensory">

**Aroma**
- Authentic Pabnar Gawa Ghee has a **deep, nutty, cooked-butter aroma** — warm, inviting, and unmistakably dairy in origin.
- The aroma should be **noticeably strong** even at room temperature and intensify considerably when a small amount is heated. If the smell is faint, flat, or neutral, it is a red flag.
- Cows grazed on the natural vegetation of Pabna's char areas often impart a **faint grassy or pastoral note** to the ghee — this is a genuine characteristic, not a defect.
- Any chemical, perfumed, coconut-like, or vegetable-oil smell indicates adulteration or artificial flavoring.

**Taste**
- The flavor should be **rich, full, and distinctly nutty** with a clean, lasting finish. There is a natural mild sweetness from the caramelized milk solids absorbed during the slow-cooking process.
- There should be **absolutely no saltiness** — pure ghee contains no salt.
- **No greasy, waxy, or soapy aftertaste.** These strongly suggest vegetable shortening, stearic acid, or animal tallow blending.
- Rancid or sour taste means the ghee is old, improperly stored, or was produced from poor-quality milk or reprocessed fat.

**Mouthfeel**
- Melts cleanly and completely on the tongue with no gritty residue. Natural crystalline granules (from solidification) are normal in solid form but should dissolve smoothly when eaten.
- Should not leave a thick, coating, or persistently oily film — this is a marker of hydrogenated fat.

</section>

## Lab / Certification Checks

<section id="certification">

Home tests indicate probable authenticity; lab tests confirm it. The following are the standard tests applicable to this product:

**Recommended Lab Tests**

| Test | What It Detects | Method |
|---|---|---|
| **Reichert-Meissl (RM) Value** | Core cow ghee identity test. Pure cow ghee: RM value 26–32. Lower values indicate vegetable fat blending | Titrimetric — BSTI/FSSAI standard |
| **Polenske Value** | Complements RM value; helps distinguish cow ghee from coconut or palm oil adulteration | Titrimetric |
| **Baudouin Test** | Detects sesame oil — a common adulterant | Add furfural + HCl; pink/red color = sesame oil present |
| **Halphen Test** | Detects cottonseed oil | Red color on heating with amyl alcohol + elemental sulfur |
| **Moisture Content** | Pure ghee: ≤0.5% moisture (BSTI standard). Higher values indicate water addition or poor processing | Karl Fischer titration or oven-drying |
| **Free Fatty Acid (FFA)** | Indicates rancidity or reprocessed old fat. FFA >0.5% is a quality concern | Titration |
| **Phytosterol / Cholesterol ratio** | Distinguishes animal fat from plant-based fat adulterants | Gas chromatography (GC) |
| **Starch / Carbohydrate test** | Detects flour or starch addition (a low-cost weight adulterant) | Iodine test — blue/purple color = starch present |

**Certifications to verify:**
- **BSTI mark** should be present on all commercially sold ghee in Bangladesh. Verify it is genuine (embossed or printed, not a sticker overlay).
- Batch number, production date, and expiry date must be clearly and permanently printed on packaging.
- Ask the seller for sourcing documentation: which region/farms, collection date, processing date.

> ⚠️ "Pure," "Khati," or "Gawa" in a product name is a claim, not a certification. Only BSTI marking and lab results constitute verifiable proof.

</section>

## Common Fakes to Watch For

<section id="fakes">

Gawa ghee adulteration is widespread in Bangladesh. These are the most commonly used methods:

**1. Dalda (Hydrogenated Vegetable Fat) Blending**
The most common adulteration by far. Dalda is inexpensive, solid at room temperature, and visually similar to ghee when blended.
- *How to spot:* Texture is too smooth and creamy when solid — no granular crystals. Aroma is significantly weaker. Palm-melt test is slow or incomplete. RM value in lab will fall well below 26.

**2. Buffalo Ghee Substitution**
Buffalo ghee is cheaper than cow ghee and frequently substituted or blended. It does not carry the same flavor profile or nutritional composition.
- *How to spot:* Color is noticeably whiter or paler. Aroma is milder and less complex. Solidifies smoothly without granular texture. Phytosterol panel and RM value will differ from cow ghee norms.

**3. Coconut Oil or Palm Oil Addition**
Liquid oils are added to increase volume and reduce cost. May not be detectable by smell alone if used in small quantities.
- *How to spot:* Melted ghee appears thinner and more fluid than normal. A faint coconut or vegetable-oil note may be detectable on close inspection. Polenske value and phytosterol tests will flag this in a lab.

**4. Artificial Color Addition**
Turmeric or synthetic yellow food dye is added to simulate the golden color of high-quality cow ghee.
- *How to spot:* Rub on white tissue paper — artificial color bleeds visibly into the paper. Natural ghee color leaves only a light translucent oil mark with no pigment transfer. In a glass jar, look for uneven color distribution or a settled pigment layer.

**5. Artificial Ghee Flavor**
Synthetic "ghee flavor" is mixed into a neutral fat base (often palm olein or vegetable shortening) and sold as pure ghee. The smell can be convincing to an untrained nose.
- *How to spot:* The aroma smells consistent and perfumed rather than naturally complex. It does not intensify on heating the way real ghee does. RM value will be near zero. There is no granular texture.

**6. Water or Milk Solids Addition**
Adds weight with minimal cost. A simple but effective short-weight fraud.
- *How to spot:* Sputtering and bubbling when heated in a dry pan. White or chalky residue at the bottom of the jar after cooling. Moisture content above 0.5% in lab testing.

**7. Reprocessed or Rancid Fat**
Old, expired, or rancid ghee — sometimes collected from restaurants or food manufacturers — is re-melted, re-filtered, and sold as fresh product.
- *How to spot:* Off, musty, sour, or stale smell rather than the warm nutty aroma of fresh ghee. Unpleasant or sharp aftertaste. High FFA value in lab testing. May darken more than normal on heating.

**Buyer checklist before purchasing:**

- [ ] Does the packaging carry a genuine BSTI mark?
- [ ] Are the batch number, production date, and expiry clearly printed (not stickered)?
- [ ] Is the color a rich golden-yellow — not pale, white, or artificially bright?
- [ ] Is the texture granular/crystalline when solid — not smooth or creamy?
- [ ] Does it have a strong, nutty, cooked-dairy aroma — not neutral or perfumed?
- [ ] Does it melt quickly and fully on a warm palm?
- [ ] Can the seller provide sourcing details (Pabna region, deshi cows)?
- [ ] Does it remain clear with no cloudiness or residue when melted?
