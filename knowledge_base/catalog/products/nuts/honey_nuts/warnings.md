## Allergen Information

<section id="allergens">

### Declared Allergens

This product contains **tree nuts**, which are a major allergen recognised by food safety authorities worldwide.

| Allergen | Present | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Tree nuts (almonds, cashews, etc.) | ✅ Yes — primary ingredient | Severe reactions possible in sensitised individuals |
| Honey (bee product) | ✅ Yes — primary ingredient | Rare but documented allergy; distinct from infant botulism risk |
| Peanuts | ⚠️ Possible | May be present if peanuts are included in the nut mix; confirm with seller |
| Gluten | ⚠️ Cross-contact risk | May be processed in a facility handling wheat products; confirm with seller |
| Soy | ⚠️ Cross-contact risk | Confirm with seller if soy allergy is severe |
| Dairy | ⚠️ Cross-contact risk | Confirm with seller if dairy allergy is severe |

### Nut Allergy Warning

> ⚠️ **CONTAINS TREE NUTS.** Not suitable for individuals with a tree nut allergy. Tree nut allergies can cause severe or life-threatening anaphylaxis. If you carry an epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen), have it accessible before consuming this or any nut product.

### Honey Allergy

Honey allergy is uncommon but real. Symptoms can include oral allergy syndrome (itching/swelling of mouth and throat), hives, or in rare cases anaphylaxis. Individuals with known pollen allergies — particularly to **bee pollen, composite flowers (e.g. sunflowers, chrysanthemums)**, or **celery** — have a higher risk of honey sensitivity.

## Who Should Avoid

<section id="contraindications">

### ❌ Infants Under 12 Months — **Do Not Feed**

> 🚫 **This product must NOT be given to infants under 12 months of age.**

Honey — including honey used as a coating or ingredient in processed foods — can contain spores of *Clostridium botulinum*. In infants under one year, the immature gut flora cannot prevent these spores from germinating and producing botulinum toxin, which can cause **infant botulism** — a rare but potentially fatal illness.

This restriction applies to **all honey-containing products**, not just raw honey.

---

### ⚠️ Groups Who Should Exercise Caution or Consult a Doctor First

| Group | Reason for Caution |
|---|---|
| Children aged 1–3 years | Nuts are a choking hazard; honey-coated nuts especially so. Serve only under adult supervision in age-appropriate portions. Always consult a paediatrician before introducing tree nuts. |
| Individuals with tree nut allergies | Risk of severe allergic reaction including anaphylaxis. Avoid completely. |
| Individuals with bee/honey allergy | Honey is a primary ingredient. Avoid completely. |
| People with diabetes (Type 1 or Type 2) | Contains natural sugars from honey; can affect blood glucose. Consult a doctor or dietitian regarding portion size. |
| People with fructose intolerance | Honey is high in fructose. May cause digestive discomfort. |
| People with irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) on a low-FODMAP diet | Honey is a high-FODMAP food and may trigger symptoms. |
| Individuals on calorie-restricted diets | Honey nuts are energy-dense (~500–600 kcal per 100g estimated). Monitor portion carefully. |
| People with kidney disease | Nuts are relatively high in potassium and phosphorus; consult a nephrologist or dietitian about safe intake. |
| Pregnant women | Honey and nuts are generally considered safe in moderate amounts during pregnancy, but raw or unprocessed honey carries a theoretical (low) risk of bacterial contamination. Prefer honey from verified processed sources. Consult your doctor. |
| Elderly individuals with swallowing difficulties (dysphagia) | Whole nuts present a choking and aspiration risk. |

## Drug / Condition Interactions

<section id="interactions">

### Drug Interactions

| Medication / Substance | Interaction | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| **Warfarin / other anticoagulants (blood thinners)** | Almonds and cashews contain Vitamin K; large or inconsistent nut intake can interfere with INR stability. | Keep nut intake consistent day-to-day; inform your prescribing doctor. |
| **MAO Inhibitors (MAOIs)** | Certain nuts contain tyramine; high tyramine intake with MAOIs can cause hypertensive crisis. | Consult your doctor if you are on MAOIs (e.g. phenelzine, tranylcypromine). |
| **Diabetes medications (insulin, metformin, sulfonylureas)** | Honey raises blood glucose; may require dose adjustment if consumed regularly. | Monitor blood sugar and consult your doctor. |
| **Immunosuppressants** | Patients on immunosuppressive therapy should avoid honey from unverified sources due to potential microbial contamination risk. | Confirm honey is from a reliable, processed source. |

### Condition-Specific Notes

- **Nut-triggered migraine:** Some individuals report almonds or other tree nuts as migraine triggers. Monitor personal response.
- **Acne-prone skin:** High-glycaemic foods (including honey) may worsen acne in susceptible individuals when consumed in excess.
- **Oral allergy syndrome (OAS):** Individuals allergic to birch pollen may experience OAS symptoms from almonds specifically (itching/tingling in mouth and throat). This is typically mild but can escalate. Stop eating and seek medical advice if symptoms worsen.

## Safe Usage Limits

<section id="safe-limits">

### General Guidance

> The following are general population guidelines based on nutritional recommendations. Individual tolerances vary. These are not medical prescriptions.

| Population | Suggested Daily Limit | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Healthy adults | 30–40g (roughly a small handful) | Aligns with standard tree nut portion recommendations from nutrition guidelines |
| Children aged 3–12 | 10–20g | Supervise closely; ensure nuts are chewed properly |
| Children under 3 | Not recommended as whole nuts | Choking hazard; if introduced, consult a paediatrician first |
| Diabetic individuals | Consult a dietitian | Honey content affects carbohydrate load; 15–20g per serving is a conservative starting point |
| Infants under 12 months | **Zero — completely contraindicated** | See honey/botulism warning above |

### Caloric Density Note

Honey nuts are calorie-dense. A 30g portion delivers approximately **150–180 kcal** (estimated, based on typical honey-coated nut nutritional profiles). Regular consumption beyond recommended portions contributes meaningfully to total daily energy intake and added sugar load.

### Storage & Consumption Safety

- Do not consume if the packaging is damaged, the nuts smell rancid or musty, or the coating shows visible mould.
- Once opened, store in an airtight container in a cool, dry place and consume within **2–4 weeks** for optimal safety and quality.
- Do not leave open portions in humid conditions — honey coating absorbs moisture, which can accelerate microbial growth.
- **Do not feed to pets**, particularly dogs and cats. Macadamia nuts (if present in the mix) are toxic to dogs. Grapes/raisins are sometimes mixed with nut products — verify the mix does not contain these if sharing a household with pets.

</section>
